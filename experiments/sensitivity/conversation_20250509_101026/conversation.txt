To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the velocity of the flow.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \( f \). Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, which would allow us to use the formula:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Here, \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s).

Let's calculate \( Re \) and then \( f \). I need your advice on the assumptions regarding the flow regime and any necessary adjustments to our calculations. What do you think?
