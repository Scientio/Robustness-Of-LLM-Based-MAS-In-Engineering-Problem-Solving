Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)). 

Next, we will look up \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). For laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), and for turbulent flow, we might need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Given our low velocity, we suspect the flow may be laminar. 

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first to confirm the flow type and then find \(f\). Let's proceed with this approach. What do you think?
