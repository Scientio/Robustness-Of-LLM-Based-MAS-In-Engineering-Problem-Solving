To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

After calculating the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. If it’s laminar (Re < 2000), we can use \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). If it’s turbulent, we will need to use the appropriate empirical correlations or the Moody chart.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then decide how to find \(f\). What do you think?
