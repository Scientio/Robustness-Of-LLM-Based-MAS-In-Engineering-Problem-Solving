Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

We need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, which would mean we can use the formula:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number calculated as:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Here, \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then find \( f \). After that, we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

What do you all think about this approach? Any suggestions or corrections?

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. Since \( Re = 1000 \), we are at the transition between laminar and turbulent flow. We can still use \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \) for our calculations:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. Now we can use this \( f \) in the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So, the pressure loss is 0.32 Pa.
