We need to compute the pressure loss due to friction in a straight water pipe with D = 0.1 m, L = 10 m, and a flow velocity V = 0.01 m/s.

Step 1: Check the flow regime.
• Reynolds number: Re = (V · D) / ν. For water, ν ≈ 1×10⁻⁶ m²/s.
  Re = (0.01 m/s × 0.1 m) / (1×10⁻⁶ m²/s) = 1000.
Since Re < 2100, the flow is laminar.

Step 2: Determine the friction factor (f).
• In laminar flow, f = 64 / Re = 64 / 1000 = 0.064.
Note: Unlike turbulent flow (where we’d use the Colebrook equation or Moody chart), in laminar flow f is uniquely given by 64/Re. So roughness isn’t an issue here.

Step 3: Use the Darcy–Weisbach equation.
Pressure drop, ΔP = f · (L/D) · (ρV²/2), where ρ for water is ~1000 kg/m³.
• Compute L/D = 10 m / 0.1 m = 100.
• Compute dynamic pressure (ρV²/2) = 1000 × (0.01²)/2 = 1000 × 0.0001/2 = 0.05 Pa.
• Thus, ΔP = 0.064 × 100 × 0.05 = 6.4 × 0.05 = 0.32 Pa.

I suggest we use this approach given our laminar flow conditions. One pitfall to watch for is using a friction factor correlation (like Colebrook) intended for turbulent flow, which would be misleading here.
