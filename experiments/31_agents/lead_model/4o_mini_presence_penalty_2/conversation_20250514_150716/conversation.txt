To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

Let's calculate \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{(1000\, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01\, \text{m/s})(0.1\, \text{m})}{0.001\, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

With \( Re = 1000 \), this indicates laminar flow since it's below 2000. For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now let's compute \( f \):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have \( f \), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
