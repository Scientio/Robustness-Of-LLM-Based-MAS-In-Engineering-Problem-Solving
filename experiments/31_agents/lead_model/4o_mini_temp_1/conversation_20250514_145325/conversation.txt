To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the velocity of the water.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). For low velocities, the flow is likely laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number calculated as:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \).

Let's calculate \( Re \) first and then determine \( f \). Can anyone confirm that using laminar flow assumptions is appropriate here given the low velocity? What do you all think?
