\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³), and
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \( f \) depends on the flow regime, which we need to determine first. Given the low velocity of 0.01m/s, the flow is likely laminar. For laminar flow, \( f \) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

where \( Re \) (Reynolds number) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

with \( \mu \) being the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)).

Let's first calculate the Reynolds number to confirm our flow regime and then determine \( f \). What do you all think about this approach? Do you agree with the calculations and the need to focus on the laminar flow condition?
