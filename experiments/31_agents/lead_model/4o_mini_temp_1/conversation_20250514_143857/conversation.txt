To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \( D = 0.1 \) m,
- Length \( L = 10 \) m,
- Velocity \( v = 0.01 \) m/s.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is approximately \( 0.001 \) Pa·s, we can calculate \( Re \) and determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. If \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar, and we can use:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

If \( Re > 4000 \), the flow is turbulent, and \( f \) would typically be found using empirical correlations or charts.
