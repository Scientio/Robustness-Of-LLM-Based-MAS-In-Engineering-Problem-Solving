To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is expressed as:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Given the flow velocity is low, we should assess whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), and for turbulent flow, we may need the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

First, let's calculate the Reynolds number (Re):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water around \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can use \(f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064\).

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
