To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho \cdot v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (water, typically about 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

The friction factor \( f \) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and the relative roughness of the pipe. Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we should first check if the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, \( Re < 2000 \) can be used, where \( Re \) (Reynolds number) is given by:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho \cdot v \cdot D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is around 1 x 10^-3 Pa.s, we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Once we have \( Re \), we can determine \( f \):
- If \( Re < 2000 \), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).
- If \( Re \geq 2000 \), we would need additional information on roughness to calculate \( f \).
