Team,

We’re looking at a pipe with Diameter D = 0.1 m, Length L = 10 m, and water flowing at v = 0.01 m/s. Our goal is to calculate the pressure drop due to friction.

Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds number:
 Re = (v × D) / ν
 Assuming ν ≈ 1×10⁻⁶ m²/s for water, we have
  Re = (0.01 × 0.1) / 1×10⁻⁶ = 1000.
Since Re < 2100, the flow is laminar.

Step 2: Determine the friction factor f:
 For laminar flow, f = 64/Re = 64/1000 = 0.064.

Step 3: Apply the Darcy–Weisbach equation:
 Δp = f × (L/D) × (ρ/2) × v²
 With ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³,
  Δp = 0.064 × (10/0.1) × (1000/2) × (0.01)².
 Calculating step by step:
  L/D = 100,
  (ρ/2) = 500,
  v² = 0.0001,
 So, Δp = 0.064 × 100 × 500 × 0.0001 = 0.064 × 5 = 0.32 Pa.

Note on f: The friction factor is critical. In turbulent regimes we’d need an iterative solution (e.g., via the Colebrook equation), but here our low Reynolds number makes the 64/Re relation valid.

I propose this approach and answer. Does anyone have feedback or alternative considerations regarding the friction factor or any other assumptions?

Preliminary Decision: Δp ≈ 0.32 Pa.
