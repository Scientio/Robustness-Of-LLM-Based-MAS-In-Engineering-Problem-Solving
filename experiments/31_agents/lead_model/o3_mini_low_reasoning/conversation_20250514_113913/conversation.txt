##### LEAD:
Team, we have a flow problem in a pipe with diameter D = 0.1 m and length L = 10 m, where water flows at a low velocity of v = 0.01 m/s. Using the Darcy–Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

  ΔP = f · (L/D) · (ρ·v²/2),

we need to pay particular attention to the friction factor f. Let’s break it down:

1. Flow regime: The Reynolds number (Re) is
  Re = (v·D) / ν.
For water (ν ≈ 1×10⁻⁶ m²/s), Re = (0.01 m/s × 0.1 m) / (1×10⁻⁶ m²/s) = 1000. This is in the laminar regime (Re < 2000), where the friction factor can be approximated as:
  f = 64/Re = 64/1000 = 0.064.

2. Pressure loss calculation:
 – Use water density ρ ≈ 1000 kg/m³,
 – Compute (L/D) = 10 m / 0.1 m = 100,
 – And (ρ·v²/2) = 1000 × (0.01)²/2 = 1000×(0.0001/2) = 1000×0.00005 = 0.05 Pa.
Therefore, ΔP = 0.064 × 100 × 0.05 = 0.32 Pa.

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa. 

Note: Some might suggest using turbulent flow friction correlations, but since Re ≈ 1000 confirms a laminar flow, the simple relation f = 64/Re is appropriate.

Team, does anyone have input or see any potential issues with this approach?
