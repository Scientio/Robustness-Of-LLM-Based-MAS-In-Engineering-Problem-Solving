Team, we’re tasked with finding the pressure loss in a pipe with diameter D = 0.1 m, length L = 10 m, and a water flow velocity v = 0.01 m/s. We’ll use the Darcy–Weisbach equation:

  Δp = f × (L/D) × (ρv²/2)

Here, ρ (water density) is ~1000 kg/m³.

First, let’s determine the Reynolds number to decide on the appropriate friction factor f. Using the formula:

  Re = (v × D) / ν

Assuming the kinematic viscosity ν ≈ 1×10⁻⁶ m²/s, we get:

  Re = (0.01 m/s × 0.1 m) / (1×10⁻⁶ m²/s) = 1000

Since Re is about 1000, the flow is laminar (Re < 2300). For laminar flow, the friction factor is given by:

  f = 64 / Re = 64/1000 = 0.064

Now, calculate the dynamic pressure component:

  (ρv²/2) = (1000 kg/m³ × (0.01 m/s)² / 2) = (1000 × 1×10⁻⁴ / 2) = 0.05 Pa

The ratio L/D equals:

  L/D = 10 m / 0.1 m = 100

Thus, the pressure loss is:

  Δp = 0.064 × 100 × 0.05 Pa = 0.32 Pa

Key note for everyone: the friction factor f is crucial here. For laminar flows, using f = 64/Re is straightforward. However, if the flow were turbulent, we’d need an estimate from the Colebrook–White equation or Moody chart, depending on the pipe’s roughness.
