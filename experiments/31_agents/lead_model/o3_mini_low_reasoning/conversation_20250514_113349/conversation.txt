  Δp = f · (L/D) · (ρ · v²/2).

Because the water’s kinematic viscosity is about 10⁻⁶ m²/s, the Reynolds number is

  Re = (v · D) / ν = (0.01 m/s · 0.1 m) / 10⁻⁶ m²/s = 1000,
  
which puts the flow in the laminar regime (Re < 2300). For laminar flow, the friction factor f is given by:

  f = 64/Re = 64/1000 = 0.064.

Taking water’s density ρ = 1000 kg/m³, the dynamic pressure term becomes

  (ρ · v²)/2 = (1000 kg/m³ · (0.01 m/s)²)/2 = 0.05 Pa.

Thus, substituting in, we obtain:

  Δp = 0.064 · (10/0.1) · 0.05 = 0.064 · 100 · 0.05 = 0.32 Pa.

A couple of notes:
1. The low flow rate gives us a laminar flow, making f = 64/Re applicable.
2. In turbulent flow scenarios, or if roughness were significant, determining f would be more involved (using, for example, the Colebrook equation).

My proposed solution is to calculate f using the laminar formula and then apply the Darcy–Weisbach equation as above.

Team, do you agree with this approach and the emphasis on using the proper friction factor for the given flow regime, or is there any aspect we should reconsider further?
