##### LEAD:
Team, we have a water pipe with diameter D = 0.1 m and length L = 10 m, and water is flowing at 0.01 m/s. The pressure loss in such a pipe can be estimated by the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

  Δp = f · (L/D) · (ρ/2) · v²

where ρ is the water density (≈1000 kg/m³), v is the velocity, and f is the friction factor.

In our case, the flow is very slow. Let’s calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to decide if it’s laminar:

  Re = (v·D) / ν

Assuming the kinematic viscosity ν of water ≈ 1.0×10⁻⁶ m²/s,

  Re = (0.01 m/s × 0.1 m) / (1.0×10⁻⁶ m²/s) = 1000.

A Re of 1000 falls within the laminar regime, under which we use f = 64/Re. Thus:

  f = 64/1000 = 0.064

Now, plugging in the numbers:

  Δp = 0.064 × (10 m / 0.1 m) × (1000 kg/m³/2) × (0.01 m/s)²

Breakdown:

  L/D = 100,
  (ρ/2) = 500 kg/m³,
  v² = 1×10⁻⁴ m²/s².

Calculating:
  Δp = 0.064 × 100 × 500 × 1×10⁻⁴
   = 6.4 × 500 × 1×10⁻⁴
   = 3200 × 1×10⁻⁴
   = 0.32 Pa

Thus, the pressure loss is approximately 0.32 Pa.
