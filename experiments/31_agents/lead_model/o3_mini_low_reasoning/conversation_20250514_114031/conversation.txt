##### LEAD:
Team, here’s our problem summary: We need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with an inner diameter of 0.1 m, a length of 10 m, and a water flow velocity of 0.01 m/s. The standard formula we’ll use is the Darcy–Weisbach equation:

  Δp = f · (L/D) · (ρ · V²/2)

where:
 • f = friction factor,
 • L = 10 m,
 • D = 0.1 m,
 • ρ (density of water) ≈ 1000 kg/m³,
 • V = 0.01 m/s.

Given the low flow velocity and small diameter, the Reynolds number (Re = V·D/ν, with ν ≈ 1×10⁻⁶ m²/s) is approximately:

  Re = (0.01 × 0.1) / 1×10⁻⁶ = 1000.

This value suggests that the flow is likely laminar or at least in the lower transitional range. In laminar flow, the friction factor can be approximated by f = 64/Re. Hence,

  f ≈ 64/1000 = 0.064.

Let's compute the dynamic pressure term:

  (ρ · V²/2) = 1000 × (0.01)² / 2 = 1000 × 0.0001 / 2 = 0.05 Pa.

Multiplying all factors:

  Δp = 0.064 × (10/0.1) × 0.05
    = 0.064 × 100 × 0.05
    = 0.32 Pa.

So, the estimated pressure loss is about 0.32 Pascals.

Key point: The friction factor f is crucial. For laminar flow (Re < 2000) the relation f = 64/Re holds well. However, if the flow were to become turbulent (Re > 4000), we would need to use a more complex determination for f, such as the Colebrook formula. Given our calculated Reynolds number, we are safely in the laminar regime overall, but we should verify the flow conditions.
