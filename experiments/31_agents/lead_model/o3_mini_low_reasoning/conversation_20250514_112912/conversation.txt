Team, we have a 10 m long, 0.1 m diameter pipe with water flowing at 0.01 m/s. To calculate the pressure loss, we use the Darcy–Weisbach equation:

  ΔP = f · (L/D) · (½ ρ v²)

At such a low velocity, we expect laminar flow. The Reynolds number is

  Re = (v·D)/ν ≈ (0.01·0.1)/1×10⁻⁶ = 1000,

which is near the transition but for our purposes we can assume laminar conditions. In laminar flow, the friction factor is f = 64/Re, so here f ≈ 64/1000 = 0.064.

Next, using water density ρ = 1000 kg/m³:

  (½ ρ v²) = 0.5 · 1000 · (0.01)² = 0.05 Pa.

Then,
  ΔP = 0.064 · (10/0.1) · 0.05 = 0.064 · 100 · 0.05 = 0.32 Pa.

Special attention: while using f = 64/Re is valid for laminar flow, if any part of the system were turbulent the friction factor would need recalculating using an iterative approach (or an empirical formula, like Colebrook’s), and inaccuracies here can lead to significant errors. Given the low velocity and Re, f appears reliable.
