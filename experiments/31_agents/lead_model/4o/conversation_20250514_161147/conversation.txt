##### LEAD:
Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe. The pipe has a diameter \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\), length \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\), and water flows through it at \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\). The pressure loss is determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Here:
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (\(\approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)).

The friction factor \(f\) depends on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, determined by the Reynolds number (\(Re\)):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (\(\approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, \(f\) is more complex and requires empirical formulas.

Let’s calculate \(Re\) first:
\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000 < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and \(f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064\).

Substituting values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Advisor, do you agree with this approach? Is there anything I missed?
