##### LEAD:
Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with diameter \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \), length \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \), and water flow velocity \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \). The pressure loss due to friction is given by the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (\( \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \( f \) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and pipe roughness. For laminar flow (\( \text{Re} < 2000 \)), \( f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}} \), where \( \text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \) is the Reynolds number and \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (\( \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)).

Let’s calculate \( \text{Re} \) first:
\[
\text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \( \text{Re} < 2000 \), the flow is laminar, and \( f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \).

Substituting values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Simplifying:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Advisor, do you agree with this approach? Any corrections or suggestions?
