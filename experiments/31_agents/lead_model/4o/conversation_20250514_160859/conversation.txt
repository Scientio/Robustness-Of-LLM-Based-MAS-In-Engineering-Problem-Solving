Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction. The pipe has a diameter (D) of 0.1 m, a length (L) of 10 m, and water flows through it at a velocity (v) of 0.01 m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the pipe length,
- \( D \) is the pipe diameter,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \( f \) depends on the Reynolds number (\( Re \)) and the pipe's roughness. Let's calculate \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s). For \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \), \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \), and \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \( Re \) is low (laminar flow), the friction factor \( f \) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So \( f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \).

Using these values, we can now calculate \( \Delta P \). Advisor, do you agree with this approach? Should we reconsider \( f \) or any assumptions?
