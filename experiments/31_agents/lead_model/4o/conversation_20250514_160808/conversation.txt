##### LEAD:
Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \), length \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \), and water flow velocity \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \). 

The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]
where \( f \) is the friction factor, \( \rho \) is the density of water (\( \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)), and \( v \) is the flow velocity.

To determine \( f \), we need the Reynolds number (\( \text{Re} \)):
\[
\text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]
where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (\( \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)).

For \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \), \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \), \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \), and \( \mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \):
\[
\text{Re} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \( \text{Re} < 2000 \), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, \( f \) is given by:
\[
f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}}
\]
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Advisor, do you agree with this approach or suggest a different method?
