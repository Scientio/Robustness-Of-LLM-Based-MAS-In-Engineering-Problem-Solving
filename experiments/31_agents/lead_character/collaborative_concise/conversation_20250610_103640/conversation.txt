To solve this, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low (0.01m/s), the flow might be laminar. We can calculate the Reynolds number to confirm this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the appropriate formula. 

Let's collaborate on calculating \(Re\) and see if we can agree on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Once we have \(f\), we can proceed with the pressure loss calculation. What are your thoughts on this approach?
