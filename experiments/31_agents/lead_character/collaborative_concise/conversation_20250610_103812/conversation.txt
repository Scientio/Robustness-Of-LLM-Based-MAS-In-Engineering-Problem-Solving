To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Since the flow velocity is low, we should check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \( Re \) will help us with that:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and determine the friction factor accordingly. I’d like to hear your thoughts on whether we should assume laminar flow or turbulent flow based on the Reynolds number we calculate.
