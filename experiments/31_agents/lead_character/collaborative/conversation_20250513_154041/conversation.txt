Team, we have a problem to solve regarding the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor \(f\). 

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Using these values, we can compute \(Re\).

2. Depending on the value of \(Re\), we can then determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we will need to use the appropriate correlations or charts.

Once we have \(f\), we can plug it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
