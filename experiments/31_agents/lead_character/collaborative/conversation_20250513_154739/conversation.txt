Team, we are tasked with calculating the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 m and a length of 10 m, where water is flowing with a velocity of 0.01 m/s. To solve this problem, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is quite low at 0.01 m/s, we will likely have laminar flow. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is approximately 0.001 Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\).

Let’s first calculate the Reynolds number:

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   - \(Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000\)

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we confirm that the flow is indeed laminar.

2. Now, calculate the friction factor \(f\):
   - \(f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064\)

3. Now we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):
   - \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}\)
   - \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}\)
   - \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05\)
   - \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\)

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
