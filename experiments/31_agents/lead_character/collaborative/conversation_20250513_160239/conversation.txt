To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Now, the tricky part here is determining the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can depend on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow, it can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

First, we should calculate the Reynolds number to see if our flow is laminar or turbulent. Let's work this out together.

2. Based on the value of \(Re\), determine whether we should use the laminar or turbulent flow equations to find \(f\).
3. Finally, we can plug all of this back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
