To solve this problem, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m続),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Given our values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m続}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Let's calculate \(Re\):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m続} \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000 \]

Since \(Re < 2000\), this indicates that the flow is laminar. In laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Substituting our value of \(Re\):

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m続} \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2} \]

3. Now substituting into the equation:
   \[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pascals.
