To approach this problem, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}\)). 

Using the values we have:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}\)

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3) \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s}) \cdot (0.1 \, \text{m})}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}} = 1000
\]

With a Reynolds number of 1000, we are at the transition between laminar and turbulent flow. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Thus, we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute this value back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[

2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
