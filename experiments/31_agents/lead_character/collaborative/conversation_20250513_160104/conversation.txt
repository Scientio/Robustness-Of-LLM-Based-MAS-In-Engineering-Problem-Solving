To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or empirical formulas, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given our low velocity, we should check if the flow is laminar.

To determine if the flow is laminar, we can calculate the Reynolds number (Re):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)). 

Now, let’s calculate the Reynolds number. 

1. **Calculate \(Re\)**:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(V = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is indeed laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

2. **Calculate \(f\)**:
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

3. **Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Let's calculate that step by step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
