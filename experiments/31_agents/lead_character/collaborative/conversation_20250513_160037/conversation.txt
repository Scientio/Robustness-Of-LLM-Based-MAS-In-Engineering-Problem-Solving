Alright team, we have an engineering problem to solve regarding pressure loss in a pipe. We have a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters. The water is flowing through this pipe at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Now, our next step is to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor depends on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given our flow velocity of 0.01 m/s, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime.

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and see which regime we are in. If it's less than 2000, the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If it's greater than 4000, we would need to use a different method to find \(f\), typically involving the Moody chart. 

So, team, let’s work together to calculate the Reynolds number and determine the friction factor. Once we have that, we can proceed to find the pressure loss. What do you all think? Any insights on how to approach this?
