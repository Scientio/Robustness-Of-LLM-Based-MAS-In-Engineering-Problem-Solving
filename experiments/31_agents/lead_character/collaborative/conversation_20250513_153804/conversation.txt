To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

The friction factor \(f\) is crucial for our calculations. It can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we should first check if the flow is laminar.

We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we have laminar flow. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting our Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute all known values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

In conclusion, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
