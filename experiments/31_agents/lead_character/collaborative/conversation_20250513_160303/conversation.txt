Alright team, we have an engineering problem to tackle regarding the pressure loss in a pipe. The parameters we have are:

- Pipe diameter (D) = 0.1 m
- Pipe length (L) = 10 m
- Water flow velocity (v) = 0.01 m/s

To determine the pressure loss due to friction in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

Now, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can depend on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given our flow velocity of 0.01 m/s, we should first calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to see which regime we are in:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting in our Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
