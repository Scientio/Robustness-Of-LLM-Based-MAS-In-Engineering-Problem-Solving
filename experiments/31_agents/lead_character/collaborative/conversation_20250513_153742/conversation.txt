Alright team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with the following specifications: diameter (D) of 0.1 meters, length (L) of 10 meters, and a flow velocity of 0.01 meters per second. 

To solve this problem, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor (f). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water, approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

1. \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
2. \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
3. \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
4. \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Now, substituting these values into the Reynolds number formula:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

With a Reynolds number of 1000, we can determine that the flow is at the transition between laminar and turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Thus,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss (\(\Delta P\)).

Let's calculate:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5
\]
\[
= 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
