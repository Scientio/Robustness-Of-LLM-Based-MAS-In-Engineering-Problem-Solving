To solve this problem, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

The key component we need to determine is the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

And for turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we would typically use empirical correlations or charts such as the Moody chart to find \(f\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water, approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature.

Given our parameters:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(V = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Let's calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), we are in the laminar flow regime. Thus, we can calculate the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
