Team, we are tasked with calculating the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, where the water flow velocity is 0.01 m/s. 

To solve this problem, we will need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water, approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature.

Given our parameters:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(\mu \approx 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Now, we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Thus,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

We can now plug the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now, substituting these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
