Alright team, we have a problem to solve regarding pressure loss in a pipe. We have a pipe with a diameter (D) of 0.1 meters and a length (L) of 10 meters. The water is flowing through this pipe at a velocity of 0.01 meters per second.

To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The key part of our calculation is determining the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor depends on the flow regime, which can be laminar or turbulent. 

1. If the flow is laminar (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:
\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]
2. If the flow is turbulent (\(Re > 4000\)), we would typically use empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation or the Moody chart.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (or \(1 \, \text{mPa.s}\)), we can plug in the values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

Now, let’s calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), we are in the laminar flow regime. Therefore, we can calculate the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pascals.
