To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to find the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, which depend on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we can calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can plug in the values:

\[
Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\):

\[

\]
