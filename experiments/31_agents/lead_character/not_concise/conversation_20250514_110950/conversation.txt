To start, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Next, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, which depend on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Given that our flow velocity is quite low (0.01 m/s), we can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, this indicates laminar flow. In laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So we can calculate \(f\) as follows:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can plug in the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\).

3. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\).
4. Now plug these into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
