To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, it's approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

A crucial part of this calculation is determining the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be determined using the Reynolds number and the Moody chart, or through empirical correlations such as the Colebrook-White equation for turbulent flow.

Since the flow velocity is quite low, we need to check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number (Re) is calculated as follows:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the appropriate friction factor. 

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Based on the Reynolds number, decide if the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000).
