To determine the pressure loss, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

The tricky part here is determining the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can depend on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, \(f\) is more complex and can be estimated using empirical correlations like the Moody chart.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (or \(1 \, \text{mPa.s}\)), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime. Thus, we can use the laminar flow friction factor equation:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(V\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

1. Substitute values:
   - \(L = 10 \, m\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
   - \(V = 0.01 \, m/s\)

2. Calculate \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating it step by step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pascals.
