To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Reynolds number \(Re\) and the relative roughness of the pipe. The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Given our flow velocity of 0.01 m/s, we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation, which could be the Moody chart or empirical formulas depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.
