First, we need to understand that pressure loss in a pipe can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Next, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be calculated using different methods, but for laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), it can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

For turbulent flow, we would typically use empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation, but given our low flow velocity, we should first calculate the Reynolds number to see if we are in the laminar regime.

The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first, then determine whether we are in the laminar or turbulent regime, and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\).
