To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or empirical correlations. Since the flow velocity is quite low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, where the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, defined as:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. Given:
- \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\mu \approx 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\).

Now, can anyone help me calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor? Once we have that, we can calculate the pressure loss in the pipe.
