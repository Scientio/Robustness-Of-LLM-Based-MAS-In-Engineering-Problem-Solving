To find the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

The challenge here is to determine the friction factor \(f\), which depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and the roughness of the pipe. 

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Let’s calculate \(Re\) to see if the flow is laminar or turbulent. If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[

\]

If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\).
