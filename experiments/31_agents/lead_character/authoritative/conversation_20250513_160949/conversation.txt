To solve this problem, we will utilize the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Given our flow velocity of 0.01 m/s, we can calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where:
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s} \) at room temperature).

After calculating \( Re \), we can determine whether the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000). This will guide us in selecting the appropriate method to find \( f \).

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Now substituting these values:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
