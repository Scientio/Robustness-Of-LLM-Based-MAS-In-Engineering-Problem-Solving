To determine the pressure loss, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is expressed as:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\) at room temperature).

Given our flow velocity (v = 0.01 m/s), we can calculate the Reynolds number and assess whether the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000). 

For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, the friction factor is more complex and typically requires empirical correlations or the Moody chart.
