Team, we have a problem to solve regarding pressure loss in a pipe. The pipe has a diameter (D) of 0.1 meters and a length (L) of 10 meters. Water is flowing through the pipe at a velocity (v) of 0.01 m/s. Our goal is to calculate the pressure loss due to friction along the length of the pipe.

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor (f). The Reynolds number is calculated as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1.002 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

We can calculate the Reynolds number with the values we have. If Re is less than 2000, the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If Re is greater than 4000, the flow is turbulent, and we would need to use the Moody chart or appropriate empirical correlations to find f.

Now, I would like to hear your thoughts on how we should proceed, especially regarding the calculation of the Reynolds number and the determination of the friction factor. What do you think?
