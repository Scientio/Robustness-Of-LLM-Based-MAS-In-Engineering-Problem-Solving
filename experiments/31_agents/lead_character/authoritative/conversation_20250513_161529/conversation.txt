Team, we have been tasked with calculating the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, where the water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01 meters per second. 

To solve this problem, we need to consider the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we need to check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \( Re \) is calculated as follows:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water at room temperature is approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \) (or \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{kg/(m·s)} \)), we can calculate \( Re \):

\[ Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m³})(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000 \]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

1. Convert the diameter \( D \) to meters, which is already done (0.1 m).
2. Substitute \( L = 10 \, m \), \( D = 0.1 \, m \), \( \rho = 1000 \, kg/m³ \), and \( v = 0.01 \, m/s \) into the equation:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \( \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100 \)
2. \( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \)

Now, substituting into the equation:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
