To the team, we have been tasked with calculating the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, given a flow velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To solve this problem, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to find the appropriate friction factor (\(f\)). The Reynolds number is calculated as follows:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Given our values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu \approx 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

We can calculate \(Re\) first, and then determine \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). 

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we would typically use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\).
