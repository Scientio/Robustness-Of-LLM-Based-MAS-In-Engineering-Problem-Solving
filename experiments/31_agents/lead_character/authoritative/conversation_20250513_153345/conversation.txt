To approach this problem, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the velocity of water.

The friction factor \(f\) depends on the flow regime, which can be either laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we need to check whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

To determine the flow regime, we can calculate the Reynolds number (Re):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

After calculating the Reynolds number, we can determine the appropriate friction factor:
- For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we would need to use empirical correlations or charts to find \(f\).
