To determine the pressure loss, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

The key part of this equation is the friction factor \(f\), which depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and the roughness of the pipe. 

Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we need to check whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated using:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\) at room temperature). 

Let’s calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime. If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we will need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\).
