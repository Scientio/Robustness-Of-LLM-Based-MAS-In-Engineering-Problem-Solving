Alright team, we have a problem to solve regarding pressure loss in a pipe with specific dimensions and flow characteristics. The pipe has a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, with water flowing through it at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To determine the pressure loss, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

Now, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be calculated using the Moody chart or relevant empirical correlations. For laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

For turbulent flow, we would use different methods such as the Colebrook-White equation, but given our flow velocity, we should first check the Reynolds number to see if we are in the laminar or turbulent regime.

The Reynolds number is given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\):

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000 \]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can use the laminar flow equation for \(f\):

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

Calculating this gives us:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
