To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 m\)
- Length \(L = 10 m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Once we calculate the Reynolds number, we can find the appropriate friction factor \(f\) using either the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.
