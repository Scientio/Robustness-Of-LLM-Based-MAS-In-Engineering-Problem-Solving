To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss.
- \(f\) is the friction factor.
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m).
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m).
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³).
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to find the friction factor. The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water around 0.001 Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlations (e.g., Moody chart).

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, then find \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, and finally apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
