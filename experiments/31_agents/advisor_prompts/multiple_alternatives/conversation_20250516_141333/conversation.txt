\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime, which will help us find the friction factor \(f\).

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Next, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent:
- If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and \(f\) can be calculated as \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we would use empirical correlations to find \(f\).
