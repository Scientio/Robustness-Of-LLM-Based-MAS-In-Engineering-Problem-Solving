To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For \(Re = 1000\), we can assume the flow is transitional. A common approximation for the friction factor in this range is the Blasius equation:

\[
f = 0.079 \cdot Re^{-0.25}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = 0.079 \cdot (1000)^{-0.25} \approx 0.079 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.014
\]

Now, we can plug in the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
3. \(\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.070\)

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is approximately \(0.070 \, Pa\).
