To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10 m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1 m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent).

Let's proceed to calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\).
