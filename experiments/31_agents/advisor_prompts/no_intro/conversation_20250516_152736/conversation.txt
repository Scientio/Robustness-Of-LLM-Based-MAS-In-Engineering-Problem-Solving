First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime, which will help us find the friction factor \( f \). The Reynolds number \( Re \) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s).

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\( Re < 2000 \)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart) to find \( f \).

Once we have \( f \), we can calculate the pressure loss \( \Delta P \) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where \( L \) is the length of the pipe, \( D \) is the diameter, \( \rho \) is the density, and \( v \) is the velocity.
