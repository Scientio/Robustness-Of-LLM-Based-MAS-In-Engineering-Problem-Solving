To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we may be dealing with laminar flow, which means we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then \(f\). After that, we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
