To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of the fluid (kg/m³), for water approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \( D = 0.1 \) m
- \( L = 10 \) m
- \( v = 0.01 \) m/s

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\( \mu \)) of water is approximately \( 0.001 \) Pa·s, we can calculate Re.

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can find the appropriate friction factor \( f \). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \). For turbulent flow, we would use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.
