Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m, a length of 10m, and a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (for laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\); for turbulent flow, we might use the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number and then decide on the friction factor. Any suggestions or insights on this approach?
