The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Since the Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this, we calculate it as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Given our parameters, let's calculate \(Re\) and determine the flow regime. 

I suggest we first calculate \(Re\) and then decide how to find \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. What do you all think?
