To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime and the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\).

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations based on \(Re\).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), we are in the laminar flow regime. Thus, we can use the proposed formula:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]
