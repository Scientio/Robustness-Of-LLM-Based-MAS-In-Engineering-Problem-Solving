The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given the low velocity, we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \). The Reynolds number (Re) can help us with this:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \) at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first to decide if we are in the laminar or turbulent regime. 

I suggest we calculate Re and then determine \( f \) accordingly. What do you all think?
