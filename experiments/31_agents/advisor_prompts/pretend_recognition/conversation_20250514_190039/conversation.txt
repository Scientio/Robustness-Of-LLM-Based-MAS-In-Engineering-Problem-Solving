To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, which means we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water at room temperature, \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then the friction factor. After that, we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
