To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

First, we need to find the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and the appropriate friction factor. 

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate Re. 

Next, we can determine whether the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000) to find the friction factor \(f\):
- For laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart).
