The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Given our flow velocity, we can calculate \(Re\) and determine whether the flow is laminar (\(Re < 2000\)) or turbulent (\(Re > 4000\)). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Let's calculate \(Re\) first and then decide on the appropriate friction factor. Please provide your insights on this approach and any calculations you might have.
