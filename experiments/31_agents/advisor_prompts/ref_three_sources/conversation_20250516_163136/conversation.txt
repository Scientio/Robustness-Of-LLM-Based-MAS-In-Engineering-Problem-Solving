To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000\) kg/m³.

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001\) Pa·s at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we will determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts, but given the low velocity, we expect laminar flow.
