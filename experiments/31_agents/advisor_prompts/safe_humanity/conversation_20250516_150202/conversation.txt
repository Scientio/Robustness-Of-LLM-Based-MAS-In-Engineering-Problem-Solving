To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\). 

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) and check if it indicates laminar flow (Re < 2000) or turbulent flow (Re > 4000). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
