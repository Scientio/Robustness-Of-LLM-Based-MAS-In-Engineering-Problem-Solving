\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Given the low flow velocity, we should consider whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated using:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\).
