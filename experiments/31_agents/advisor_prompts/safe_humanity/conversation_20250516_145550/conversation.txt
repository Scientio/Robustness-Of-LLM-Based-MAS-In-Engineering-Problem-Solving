##### LEAD:
Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or charts like the Moody chart to find \(f\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and decide on the friction factor. What do you all think?
