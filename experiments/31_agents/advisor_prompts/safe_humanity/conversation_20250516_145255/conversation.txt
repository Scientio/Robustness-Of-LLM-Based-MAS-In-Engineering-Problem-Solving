To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \( D = 0.1 \) m,
- Length \( L = 10 \) m,
- Flow velocity \( v = 0.01 \) m/s.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is approximately \( 0.001 \) Pa·s, we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Next, we'll find the appropriate value for the friction factor \( f \) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \( f \) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we may need to use empirical correlations or the Moody chart.
