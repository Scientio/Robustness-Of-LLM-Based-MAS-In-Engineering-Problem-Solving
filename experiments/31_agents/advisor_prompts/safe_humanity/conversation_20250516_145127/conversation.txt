To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.
