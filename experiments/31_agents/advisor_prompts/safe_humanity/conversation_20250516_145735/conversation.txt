Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of flow.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Given the low velocity, I suspect the flow is laminar, which means we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then find \(f\). Any thoughts on the calculations or the approach?
