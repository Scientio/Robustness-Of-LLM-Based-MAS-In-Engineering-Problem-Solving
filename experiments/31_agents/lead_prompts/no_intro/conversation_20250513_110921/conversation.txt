##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is considered transitional, and we can use empirical correlations to estimate \(f\). A common approximation for transitional flow is:

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot Re^{-0.25}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot 1000^{-0.25} \approx 0.079 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.014
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.070
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.070 Pa.

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.070 Pa.
