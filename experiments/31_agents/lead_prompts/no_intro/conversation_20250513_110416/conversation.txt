##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) is the friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (kg/m続)
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (m/s)

### Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m続\)

### Steps to Solve:
1. **Calculate the Reynolds number (Re)** to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]
   (Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water at room temperature is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\)).

2. **Determine the friction factor \(f\)** based on the Reynolds number. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]
   For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts like the Moody chart.

3. **Substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation** to find \(\Delta P\).
