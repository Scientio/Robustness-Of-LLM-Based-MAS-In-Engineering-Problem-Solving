##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1\) m
- Length \(L = 10\) m
- Velocity \(v = 0.01\) m/s
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000\) kg/m³

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001\) Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, depending on the value of \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations (like the Colebrook equation for turbulent flow or the Hagen-Poiseuille equation for laminar flow).

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\).
