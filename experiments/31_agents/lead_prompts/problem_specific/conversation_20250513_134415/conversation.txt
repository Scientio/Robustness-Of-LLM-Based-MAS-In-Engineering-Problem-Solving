Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \( f \), we can use the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation, but since the flow velocity is quite low, we may expect laminar flow, where \( f \) can be calculated as:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water around \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \).

Let's calculate \( Re \) first and then find \( f \). I’d like your input on the assumptions about the flow regime and if we should consider any additional factors. What do you think?
