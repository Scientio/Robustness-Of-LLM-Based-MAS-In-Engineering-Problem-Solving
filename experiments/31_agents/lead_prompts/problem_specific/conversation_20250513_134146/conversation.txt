To determine the pressure loss, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Since we need to find the friction factor \( f \), we can use the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation, but we should first determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \), we can calculate \( Re \) and decide whether we are in the laminar regime (Re < 2000) or turbulent regime (Re > 4000).
