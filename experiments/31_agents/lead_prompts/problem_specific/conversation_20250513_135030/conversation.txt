To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the low velocity, the flow is likely laminar. We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can check if \(Re < 2000\) for laminar flow. 

If \(Re\) indicates laminar flow, we can use:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

I would like your input on the calculations, particularly the Reynolds number and the friction factor. What do you think?
