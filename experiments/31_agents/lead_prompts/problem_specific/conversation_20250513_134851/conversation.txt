Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\), which depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). 

Given that the flow velocity is quite low, we should check if the flow is laminar (Re < 2000). The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about 0.001 Pa·s, let's calculate \(Re\) and determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Once we know the flow regime, we can find \(f\) using appropriate correlations. 

I’d like your input on calculating the Reynolds number and determining the friction factor from there. What do you think?
