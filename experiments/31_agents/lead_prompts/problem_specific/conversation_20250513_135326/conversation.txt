Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \( f \), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we should calculate the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) for water of about 0.001 Pa·s, we can calculate \( Re \) and use it to find \( f \) from the appropriate correlation (likely the Moody chart for turbulent flow or directly \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \) for laminar flow).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime and then proceed to find the friction factor. What do you think?
