To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states that the pressure loss due to friction in a pipe is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given the low velocity, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water around 0.001 Pa.s, let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\).
