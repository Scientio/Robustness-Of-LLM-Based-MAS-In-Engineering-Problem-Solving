Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Reynolds number \(Re\) and the relative roughness of the pipe. Since the flow velocity is low, we should expect laminar flow, where \(Re < 2000\). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we are in the laminar regime, and the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

I suggest we proceed with this calculation. Does anyone have any concerns or suggestions regarding the friction factor or the assumptions we made?
