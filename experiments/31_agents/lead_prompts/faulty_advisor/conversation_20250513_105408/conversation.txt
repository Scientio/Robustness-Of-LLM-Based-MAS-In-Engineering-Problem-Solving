To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate Re.

After finding Re, we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate empirical correlations (like the Moody chart for turbulent flow or the Hagen-Poiseuille equation for laminar flow). 

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
