To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for head loss due to friction:

\[ 
h_f = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{v^2}{2g} 
\]

Where:
- \( h_f \) is the head loss due to friction,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( v \) is the flow velocity, and
- \( g \) is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.81 m/s²).

Since the flow velocity is low, we may need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, as this will affect the friction factor \( f \).

The Reynolds number \( Re \) is given by:

\[ 
Re = \frac{ρ \cdot v \cdot D}{μ} 
\]

Where:
- \( ρ \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( μ \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s).

For laminar flow (\( Re < 2000 \)), the friction factor \( f \) can be calculated as:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.
