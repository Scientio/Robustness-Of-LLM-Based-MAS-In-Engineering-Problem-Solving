The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

To find the friction factor \( f \), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. We can calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)). 

If \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar, and we can use:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

If \( Re > 4000 \), the flow is turbulent, and we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \( f \).

Given our parameters, let's calculate \( Re \) and determine the appropriate friction factor.
