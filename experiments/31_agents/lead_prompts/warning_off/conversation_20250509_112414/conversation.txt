Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, carrying water at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\) and identify whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Next, for laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we can use empirical correlations such as the Colebrook-White equation.
