Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can plug in our values to find \(Re\).

Once we know \(Re\), we can determine \(f\):
- If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we can use empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and determine the appropriate \(f\). What do you think about this approach? Any suggestions on refining our calculations?
