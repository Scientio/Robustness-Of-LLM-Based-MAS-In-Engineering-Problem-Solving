To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss due to friction:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa s}\). 

Next, we can categorize the flow as laminar or turbulent based on the Reynolds number. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or the Moody chart.

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime and then find the appropriate friction factor. What do you think? Any further suggestions or considerations?
