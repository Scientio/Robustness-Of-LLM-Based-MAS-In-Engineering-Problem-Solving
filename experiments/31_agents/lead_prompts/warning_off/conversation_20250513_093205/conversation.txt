To calculate the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s at room temperature).

Next, we can determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent:
- Laminar if \(Re < 2000\),
- Turbulent if \(Re > 4000\).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can use the appropriate correlation to find \(f\):
- For laminar flow: \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\),
- For turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Given the low velocity, it is likely that we will have laminar flow.
