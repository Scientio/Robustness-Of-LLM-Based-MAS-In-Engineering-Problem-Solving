Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate Re.

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation to find \(f\). If Re < 2000, we can use the laminar flow equation:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

If Re > 4000, we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations for turbulent flow.

After determining \(f\), we can plug it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. What do you think? Any alternative approaches or considerations we should keep in mind?
