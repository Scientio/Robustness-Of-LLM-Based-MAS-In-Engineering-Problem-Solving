\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \left( \frac{L}{D} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \right) 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart or empirical equations) depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Given the low velocity, we suspect the flow may be laminar. In laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

After calculating \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
