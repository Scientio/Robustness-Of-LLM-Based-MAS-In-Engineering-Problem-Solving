To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate Re.

Next, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we may need to use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart).

Given the low velocity, we should expect laminar flow. 

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first to confirm the flow regime and then compute \(f\) accordingly.
