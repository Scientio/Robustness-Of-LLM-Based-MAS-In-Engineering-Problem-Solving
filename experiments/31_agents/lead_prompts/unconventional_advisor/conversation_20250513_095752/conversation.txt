\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa s}\), we can calculate \(Re\). 

Once we have \(Re\), we can use empirical correlations (like the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation) to find \(f\).
