Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for head loss due to friction:

\[ 
h_f = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{v^2}{2g} 
\]

Where:
- \( h_f \) is the head loss due to friction,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( v \) is the flow velocity, and
- \( g \) is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.81 m/s²).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ 
Re = \frac{D \cdot v}{\nu} 
\]

Where \( \nu \) is the kinematic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-6} m²/s \) at room temperature).

Once we calculate the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000). For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we can use empirical correlations like the Colebrook-White equation.

After determining \( f \), we can substitute back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the head loss. We can then convert head loss to pressure loss using:

\[ 
\Delta P = \rho \cdot g \cdot h_f 
\]

Where \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss and \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³).
