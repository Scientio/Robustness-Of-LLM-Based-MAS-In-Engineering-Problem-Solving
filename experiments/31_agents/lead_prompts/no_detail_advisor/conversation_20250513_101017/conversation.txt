To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Based on \(Re\), we can find the appropriate value for \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical formulas.

Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, where \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} \, \text{(for laminar flow)}
\]

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
