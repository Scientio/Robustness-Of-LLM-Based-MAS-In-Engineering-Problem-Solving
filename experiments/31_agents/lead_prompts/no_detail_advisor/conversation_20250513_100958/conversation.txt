To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can compute \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we are in the transitional flow regime. For turbulent flow, we can use the Colebrook-White equation or empirical correlations to estimate \(f\). 

As a starting point, we can use the approximation for turbulent flow:

\[
f \approx 0.3164 \cdot Re^{-0.25}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\):

\[
f \approx 0.3164 \cdot (1000)^{-0.25} \approx 0.3164 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.0563
\]

Now we can plug \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.0563 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.0563 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.0563 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.0563 \cdot 5 = 0.2815 \, \text{Pa}
