To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m,
- \(L = 10\) m,
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is transitional, and we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate the friction factor \(f\). A common approximation for turbulent flow is:

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot Re^{-0.25}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot (1000)^{-0.25} \approx 0.03
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.03 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.03 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.03 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.15 \text{ Pa}
\]
