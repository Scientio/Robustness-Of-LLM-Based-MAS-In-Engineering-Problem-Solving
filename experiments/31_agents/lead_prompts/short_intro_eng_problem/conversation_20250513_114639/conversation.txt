##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
- Length \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \)
- Flow velocity \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)

To find the friction factor \( f \), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. We can calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \( f \) can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Now, let's calculate \( f \):

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we have everything we need to calculate the pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

Calculating \( \Delta P \):

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \]
