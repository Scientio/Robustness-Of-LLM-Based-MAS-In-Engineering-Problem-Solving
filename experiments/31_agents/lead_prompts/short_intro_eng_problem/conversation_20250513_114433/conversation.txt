##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

With a Reynolds number of 1000, we are likely in the transition regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation to estimate \(f\). For simplicity, let's assume \(f \approx 0.02\) for turbulent flow.

Now, we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.1 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Now I would like to ask the team if they agree with the assumptions made, particularly regarding the friction factor \(f\) and the flow regime. What do you think? 

Preliminary Decision: Pressure loss is approximately 0.1 Pa.
