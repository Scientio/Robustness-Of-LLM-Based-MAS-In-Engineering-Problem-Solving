To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we typically refer to the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

After calculating \(f\), we can substitute all known values back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
