To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Use the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\) (can be obtained from the Moody chart or empirical correlations).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
