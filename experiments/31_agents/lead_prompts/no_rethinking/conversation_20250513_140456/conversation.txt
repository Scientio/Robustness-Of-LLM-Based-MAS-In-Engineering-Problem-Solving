Let's break down the problem of calculating pressure loss in a pipe. We have a pipe with a diameter (D) of 0.1m and a length (L) of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity (v) of 0.01m/s. 

To determine the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Use the appropriate method to find the friction factor \(f\) (e.g., Moody chart or empirical correlations).
3. Substitute back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

The Reynolds number can be calculated as follows:

\[
