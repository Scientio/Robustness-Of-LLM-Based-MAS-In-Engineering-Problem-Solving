To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the friction factor \(f\).

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water around 0.001 Pa·s, we can calculate Re.

Once we know Re, we can use the appropriate correlation to find \(f\):
- For laminar flow (Re < 2000): \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\)
- For turbulent flow (Re > 4000): we use the Colebrook equation or similar.
