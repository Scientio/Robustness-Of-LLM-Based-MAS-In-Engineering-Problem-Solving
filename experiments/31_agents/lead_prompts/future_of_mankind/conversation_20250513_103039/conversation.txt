The pressure loss due to friction in a pipe can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. If \(Re < 2000\), we have laminar flow, and we can use the formula:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

If \(Re > 4000\), we have turbulent flow, and we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\).

Let’s calculate the Reynolds number first and see if we are in the laminar or turbulent regime. Based on that, we can proceed to calculate the pressure loss.
