\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (approximately 1000 kg/m³ for water),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) is about \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s.

Next, we will determine the friction factor \( f \) based on the flow regime:
- For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).
- For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we can use the Colebrook equation or empirical correlations.
