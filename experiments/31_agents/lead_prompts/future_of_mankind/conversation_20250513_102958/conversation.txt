To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{D \cdot v \cdot \rho}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[ Re = \frac{0.1 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 1000}{0.001} = 1000 \]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we are in the transition between laminar and turbulent flow. The friction factor \(f\) can be approximated using the following relation for laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Thus, 

\[ f \approx \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
