The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can compute \(Re\) and determine \(f\) accordingly. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or the Moody chart.

Given the low velocity, we are likely in the laminar flow regime. 

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, find \(f\), and then proceed to calculate \(\Delta P\). What do you think about this approach? Any other considerations we should take into account?
