Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\). 

Given our parameters:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m³}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

Let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\). After that, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

I would like your input on the calculations and if there are any other considerations we should take into account regarding the flow regime or the pipe material that might affect the friction factor.
