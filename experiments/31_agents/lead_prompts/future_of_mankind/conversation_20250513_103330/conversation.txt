To solve this problem, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate the friction factor. For turbulent flow, a common approximation is:

\[ f \approx 0.3164 \cdot Re^{-0.25} \]
