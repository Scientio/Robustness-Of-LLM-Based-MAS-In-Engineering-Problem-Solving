Team, we have a critical engineering problem to solve regarding pressure loss in a pipe. The specifications are as follows: the pipe diameter is 0.1m, the length is 10m, and the water flow velocity is 0.01m/s.

To determine the pressure loss, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we should first check if the flow is laminar, which is indicated by a Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\).

Let's calculate \(Re\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is indeed laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor for laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now, substituting the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

1. Calculate \(\Delta P\):
   \[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

2. Simplifying:
   \[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} \]
   \[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
   \[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \]

This gives us the pressure loss in the pipe.
