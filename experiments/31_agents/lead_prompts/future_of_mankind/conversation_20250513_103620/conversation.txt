Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

The friction factor \(f\) is crucial here and depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). Given the low velocity, we should first determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then decide on the appropriate method to find \(f\).

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first and then determine \(f\) accordingly. What are your thoughts on this approach?

\[

\]
