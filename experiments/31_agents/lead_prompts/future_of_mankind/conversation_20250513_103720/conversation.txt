Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this problem, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for head loss due to friction:

\[ h_f = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{v^2}{2g} \]

Where:
- \( h_f \) is the head loss (m)
- \( f \) is the friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (m/s)
- \( g \) is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.81 m/s²)

The friction factor \( f \) can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given our low flow velocity, we should first check if the flow is laminar, which occurs when the Reynolds number \( Re < 2000 \).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming water at room temperature (\( \rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) and \( \mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

Since \( Re = 1000 \) indicates laminar flow, we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute \( f \) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \( h_f \):

\[ h_f = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{(0.01)^2}{2 \cdot 9.81} \]

Calculating \( h_f \):

\[ h_f = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{0.0001}{19.62} \]

\[ h_f \approx 0.000325 \, \text{m} \]

To find the pressure loss \( \Delta P \), we convert head loss to pressure using:

\[ \Delta P = \rho g h_f \]
