To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \( f \). Since the flow velocity is low, we might expect laminar flow, which would mean we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \), we can calculate \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \).

Let's proceed with these calculations. Any advice on assumptions or additional factors we should consider?
