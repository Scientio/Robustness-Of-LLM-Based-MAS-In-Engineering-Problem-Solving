Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s). 

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation for \( f \) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Given the low velocity, we suspect the flow might be laminar (Re < 2000), in which case:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, then determine \( f \), and finally compute the pressure loss. What do you all think?
