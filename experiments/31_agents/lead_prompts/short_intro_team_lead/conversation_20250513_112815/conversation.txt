Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime, which will help us find the friction factor \( f \). The Reynolds number \( Re \) is given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Once we find \( Re \), we can determine \( f \) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas). 

Let's calculate \( Re \) and then discuss how to find \( f \). What are your thoughts on the approach?
