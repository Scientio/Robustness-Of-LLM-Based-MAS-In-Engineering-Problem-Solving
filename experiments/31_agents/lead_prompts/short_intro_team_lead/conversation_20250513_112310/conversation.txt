\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the low velocity, we may be in the laminar flow regime. We can calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we can use the laminar flow friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
