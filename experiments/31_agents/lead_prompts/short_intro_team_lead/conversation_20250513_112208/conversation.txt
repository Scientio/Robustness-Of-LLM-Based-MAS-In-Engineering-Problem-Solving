To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and determine if the flow is laminar (\(Re < 2000\)) or turbulent (\(Re > 4000\)). 

For laminar flow, the friction factor is given by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If the flow is turbulent, we will need to use empirical correlations to find \(f\).
