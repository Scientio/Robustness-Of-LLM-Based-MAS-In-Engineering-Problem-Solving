To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³, for water approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1\) m
- Length \(L = 10\) m
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01\) m/s

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations (like the Colebrook equation for turbulent flow).
