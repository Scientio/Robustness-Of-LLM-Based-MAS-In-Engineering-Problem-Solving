To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa.s\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can use empirical correlations to find the friction factor \(f\):
- For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow, we would use the Colebrook equation or the Moody chart, but given our expected low velocity, we might be in the laminar range.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and friction factor, then substitute into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
