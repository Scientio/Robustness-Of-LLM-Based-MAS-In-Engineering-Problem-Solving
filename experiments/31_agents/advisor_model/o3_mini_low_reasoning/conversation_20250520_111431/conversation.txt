To the team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \) at room temperature).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can use the appropriate correlations (like the Moody chart) to find \( f \).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, and then determine \( f \) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. What do you all think?
