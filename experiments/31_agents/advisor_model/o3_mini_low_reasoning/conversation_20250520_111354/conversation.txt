To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water in this case, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

To find the friction factor \(f\), we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations depending on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). Since the flow velocity is low, we may be in the laminar regime, where:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then \(f\).
