Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \( f \), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a viscosity \( \mu \) of water around 0.001 Pa.s, we can find \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \) using the appropriate correlation for laminar flow (if \( Re < 2000 \), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \)).

I suggest we calculate \( Re \), determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, and then find \( f \) accordingly. What do you all think?
