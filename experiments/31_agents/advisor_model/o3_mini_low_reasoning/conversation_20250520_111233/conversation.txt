To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate Re.

After that, we can find the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation, such as the Moody chart or empirical equations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and assess the flow regime to find the friction factor. Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
