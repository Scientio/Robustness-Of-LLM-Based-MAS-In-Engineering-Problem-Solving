To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we can assume laminar flow, where \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\).
