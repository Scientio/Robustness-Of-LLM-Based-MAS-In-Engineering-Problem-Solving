To solve the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\),
- \(L = 10 \, m\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\)).

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can find the friction factor using the appropriate correlation (e.g., for laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), and for turbulent flow, we might use the Colebrook-White equation).
