To solve the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we need to consider the flow characteristics and the friction factor. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 m\)
- Length \(L = 10 m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 kg/m³\)

We need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then find the appropriate friction factor using the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Team, I suggest we proceed with calculating the Reynolds number first to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the friction factor. What do you think?

\[
