##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

First, let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can find:

\[
Re = \frac{(1000)(0.01)(0.1)}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime, allowing us to use the formula for \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Let's calculate it:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
