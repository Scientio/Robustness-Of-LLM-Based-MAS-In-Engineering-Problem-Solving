The pressure loss in the pipe can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss,
- \( f \) = friction factor,
- \( L \) = length of the pipe,
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) = density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) = flow velocity.

We need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Given the low velocity, we suspect laminar flow, which means \( Re < 2000 \). In laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water at room temperature, \( \mu \) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first, and then find the friction factor, followed by substituting these values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to get the pressure loss.

What do you think, team? Any thoughts or suggestions on how to approach this calculation?
