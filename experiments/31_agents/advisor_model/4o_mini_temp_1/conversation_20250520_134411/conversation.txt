To determine the pressure loss, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho \cdot v^2}{2}\right)
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss
- \(f\) is the friction factor
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) is the flow velocity

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho \cdot v \cdot D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature. 

Once we find \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or appropriate correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given that the velocity is quite low, we might expect laminar flow.

Let's discuss the approach: 
1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Determine the friction factor \(f\).
3. Apply it to the Darcy-Weisbach equation.
