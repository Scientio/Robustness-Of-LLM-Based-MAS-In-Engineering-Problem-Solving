To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states that the pressure loss (ΔP) due to friction in a pipe can be calculated as:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- ΔP = pressure loss (Pa)
- f = friction factor (dimensionless)
- L = length of the pipe (m)
- D = diameter of the pipe (m)
- ρ = density of the fluid (kg/m³, for water it's about 1000 kg/m³)
- v = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor (f):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- μ = dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature)

Next, we can find the friction factor using empirical correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent:
- If Re < 2000: \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \)
- If Re ≥ 2000: use the Moody chart or Colebrook-White equation.
