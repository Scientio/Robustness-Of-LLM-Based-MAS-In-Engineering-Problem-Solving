\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = velocity of water

To proceed, we need to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the low velocity, we may be dealing with laminar flow where \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

With \(\mu\) being the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s). Given our flow conditions, I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, and then find the appropriate \(f\).
