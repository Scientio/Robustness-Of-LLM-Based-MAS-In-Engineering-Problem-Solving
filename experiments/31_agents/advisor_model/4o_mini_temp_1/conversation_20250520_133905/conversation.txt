##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The friction factor depends on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}}
\]

Where Re is the Reynolds number. For turbulent flow, \(f\) can be estimated using empirical correlations like the Moody chart.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

Let’s first calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
\text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
\text{Re} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since Re < 2000, the flow is laminar, so:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can use these values to calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

This simplifies to:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.005 = 0.032 \, Pa
\]
