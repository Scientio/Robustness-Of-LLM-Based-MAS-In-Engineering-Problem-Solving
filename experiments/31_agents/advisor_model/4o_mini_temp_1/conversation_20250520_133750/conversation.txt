To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

In our case:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000\) kg/m³.

The first step is to determine the friction factor \(f\). The flow is likely laminar due to the low velocity, so we can use the equation for laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity of water \(\mu \approx 0.001\) Pa·s. 

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then \(f\). After that, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\).
