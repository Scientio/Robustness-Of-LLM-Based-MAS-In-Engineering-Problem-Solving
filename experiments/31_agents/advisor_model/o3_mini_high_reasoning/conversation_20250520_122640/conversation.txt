Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then use it to find \(f\) from the Moody chart or appropriate equations for laminar or turbulent flow.

Given the low velocity, we may expect laminar flow (Re < 2000). In that case, \(f\) can be approximated by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let’s calculate \(Re\) first and confirm if we are in the laminar flow regime. What do you all think?
