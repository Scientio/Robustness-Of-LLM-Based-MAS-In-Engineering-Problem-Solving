To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the pipe length (10m),
- \(D\) is the pipe diameter (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to find the Reynolds number (Re) to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the friction factor \(f\).

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let’s calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
