##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

Now, let's calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Team, based on our calculations, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa. Please confirm if you agree with this approach and calculations or suggest any adjustments.
