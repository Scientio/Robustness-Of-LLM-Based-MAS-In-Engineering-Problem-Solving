The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa s}\)).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can use the Moody chart or empirical formulas to find the friction factor \(f\). 

Given the low flow velocity, we may expect laminar flow, which means:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then find the friction factor. 

I would like your input on the calculations and if anyone has suggestions on the best way to determine the friction factor for our specific scenario.
