To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we may assume laminar flow, which means we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\).

Now, let's calculate \(Re\):

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. Calculate \(f\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. Now, substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

4. Simplifying:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]
