The pressure loss due to friction in the pipe can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s).

Next, we can find the friction factor using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow, depending on the Reynolds number.

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the friction factor. What do you all think?
