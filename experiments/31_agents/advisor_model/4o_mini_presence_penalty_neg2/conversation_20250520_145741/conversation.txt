To tackle this, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the diameter and velocity, we can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Once we find \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (for laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), and for turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or an empirical correlation).
