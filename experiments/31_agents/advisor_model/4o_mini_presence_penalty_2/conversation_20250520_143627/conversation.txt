To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,

- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water at room temperature is about \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s} \), we can compute \( Re \).

Once we have \( Re \), we can decide if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us choose the correct formula for \( f \):
- For laminar flow (\( Re < 2000 \)), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).
- For turbulent flow, we may use empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation.
