To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \( D = 0.1 \) m
- Length \( L = 10 \) m
- Velocity \( v = 0.01 \) m/s

The first step is to find the Reynolds number (\( Re \)) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\( \mu \)) of water at room temperature is about \( 0.001 \) Pa·s, we calculate \( Re \).

Next, based on the value of \( Re \), we will either use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \).
