To assess the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\( Re \)) to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\( \mu \)) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \), we calculate:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, which indicates laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we substitute this back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step by step gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
