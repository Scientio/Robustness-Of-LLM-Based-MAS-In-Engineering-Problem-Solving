To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming standard conditions for water, \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity) is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

Let's calculate it together and discuss how to find \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. In laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

In turbulent flow (\(Re > 4000\)), we would typically use empirical correlations like the Moody chart.
