To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the appropriate friction factor.

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa s}\):

Let's calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), this indicates laminar flow. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now substituting these values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
