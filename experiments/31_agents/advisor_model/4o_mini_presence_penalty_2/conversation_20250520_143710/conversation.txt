##### LEAD:
Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this problem, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) for water around 0.001 Pa·s, let's calculate the Reynolds number.

After that, based on the Reynolds number, we can estimate the friction factor \( f \). For laminar flow (\( Re < 2000 \)), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \); for turbulent flow (\( Re > 4000 \)), we would use empirical correlations or charts.

Let's proceed with these calculations, starting with the Reynolds number. What do you think?
