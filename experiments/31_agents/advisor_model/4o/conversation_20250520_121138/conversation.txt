Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m, a length of 10m, and a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can use the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical equations) to find the friction factor \(f\).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the friction factor. What do you all think?

\[
