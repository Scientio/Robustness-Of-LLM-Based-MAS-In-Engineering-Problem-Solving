##### LEAD:
To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s.

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can find the appropriate friction factor using the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

While the relationship \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) is a simplification that can be used for laminar flow (Re < 2000), it doesn't account for turbulent flow or the variations in the friction factor for different flow regimes. 

We should calculate the Reynolds number to ensure we are in the correct flow regime and then select the appropriate method to determine \(f\). 

Let's proceed with calculating \(Re\) first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can indeed use the simplified formula for \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \text{ Pa}
\]

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.125 Pa.
