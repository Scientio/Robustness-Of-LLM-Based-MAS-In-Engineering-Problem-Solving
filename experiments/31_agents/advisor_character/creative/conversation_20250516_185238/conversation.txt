To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m,
- \(L = 10\) m,
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Next, we will use the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart) to find \(f\) based on the Reynolds number, which will help us calculate the pressure loss.
