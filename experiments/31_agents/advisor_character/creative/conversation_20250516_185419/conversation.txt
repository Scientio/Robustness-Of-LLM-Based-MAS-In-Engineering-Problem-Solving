To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we should consider whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, the friction factor can be more complex and often requires empirical correlations or charts.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, then determine whether we are in the laminar or turbulent regime, and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\). What do you all think?
