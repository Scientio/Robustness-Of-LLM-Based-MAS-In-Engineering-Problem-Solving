To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the water (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\). A common approximation for laminar flow (Re < 2000) is:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step by step:

1. \(\frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(0.01^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
4. \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\)
