To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can compute \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using appropriate correlations (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and friction factor, then use them to find the pressure loss.
