\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water around \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (at room temperature), we can calculate \(Re\) and then find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (either for laminar or turbulent flow).

Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, where \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\). After that, we can compute the pressure loss.
