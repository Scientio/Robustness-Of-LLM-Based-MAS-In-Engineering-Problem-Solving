The problem involves calculating the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction as water flows through it. We need to determine the friction factor \( f \) first, as it significantly influences the pressure loss.

To calculate the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approx. 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \( D = 0.1 \, m \)
- \( L = 10 \, m \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, m/s \)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \) to determine the flow regime and the appropriate friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \( \mu \) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \( 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s \). 

Next, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find \( f \):
- If \( Re < 2000 \): Laminar flow, \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \)
- If \( Re > 4000 \): Turbulent flow, we would typically use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.
