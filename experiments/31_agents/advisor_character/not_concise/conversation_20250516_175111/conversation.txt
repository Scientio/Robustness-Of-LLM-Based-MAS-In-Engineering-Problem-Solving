To address the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000\) kg/m³

To find the friction factor \(f\), we can use the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation. Since the flow velocity is quite low, we might consider laminar flow conditions, which would give us:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, defined as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001\) Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\).

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, and then find the appropriate friction factor \(f\).
