To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely transitional. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate the friction factor. A common approximation for transitional flow is:

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot Re^{-0.25}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f \approx 0.079 \cdot 1000^{-0.25} \approx 0.079 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.014
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.070
\]

So, the pressure loss is approximately 0.070 Pa.
