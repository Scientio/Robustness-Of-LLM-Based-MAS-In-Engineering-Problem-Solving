Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of water.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow. We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} \quad \text{(for laminar flow, } Re < 2000\text{)}
\]

Let's compute \(Re\) and find \(f\). What do you all think? Should we proceed with this approach?
