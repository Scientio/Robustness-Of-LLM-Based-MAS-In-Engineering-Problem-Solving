To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can find the appropriate friction factor \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations (like the Colebrook equation for turbulent flow or the Hagen-Poiseuille equation for laminar flow).

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. Once we have \(f\), we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
