To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

In our case:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

The friction factor \(f\) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). Given the low velocity, we should first determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent using the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water around \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\), we can compute \(Re\).

If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If \(Re > 4000\), we would use empirical correlations for turbulent flow.
