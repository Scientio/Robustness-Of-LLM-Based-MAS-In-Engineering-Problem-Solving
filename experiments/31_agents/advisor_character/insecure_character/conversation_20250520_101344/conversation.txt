To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of the fluid (kg/m³, for water approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \( D = 0.1 \) m
- \( L = 10 \) m
- \( v = 0.01 \) m/s

We need to find the friction factor \( f \). For laminar flow, \( f \) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \( \mu \approx 0.001 \) Pa·s. 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. I suggest we calculate \( Re \), determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, and then find the appropriate friction factor \( f \).
