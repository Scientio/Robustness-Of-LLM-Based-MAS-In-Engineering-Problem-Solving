Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the pipe length,
- \(D\) is the pipe diameter,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, defined as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa.s).

Given the low velocity, we should first check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

Let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\). What do you think about this approach?
