To solve this problem, we need to calculate the pressure loss due to friction. The Darcy-Weisbach equation is typically used for this purpose:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number (Re) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)). 

Given our parameters, we can calculate Re and then use it to find \(f\). If Re < 2000, the flow is laminar, and \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If Re > 4000, we would use a different correlation for turbulent flow.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and see if we can determine the friction factor. Team, do you agree with this approach, or do you think we should consider any other factors?
