AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

For water, the density \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³. 

First, we need to determine the Darcy friction factor \(f\). The friction factor can be found using the Moody chart or appropriate empirical correlations, which depend on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). 

Given that the flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) is quite low, we can check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) first to determine the flow regime. 

1. **Calculate Reynolds Number**:
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

2. **Calculate Friction Factor**:
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

3. **Substitute into Darcy-Weisbach Equation**:
Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\).

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is **0.32 Pa**.
