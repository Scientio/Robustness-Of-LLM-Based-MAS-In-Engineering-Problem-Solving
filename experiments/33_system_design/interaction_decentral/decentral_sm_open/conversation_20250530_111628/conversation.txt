AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

1. **Calculate \(Re\)**:
   \[
   Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
   \]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime.

2. **Calculate \(f\)**:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Now, we can calculate \(\Delta P\)**:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{(1000)(0.0001)}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
