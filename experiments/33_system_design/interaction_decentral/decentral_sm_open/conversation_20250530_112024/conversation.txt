AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number (Re)**:
   The Reynolds number helps determine the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and is calculated as:

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   For water at room temperature, we can assume:
   - Density (\(\rho\)) ≈ 1000 kg/m³
   - Dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) ≈ 0.001 Pa·s

   Plugging in the values:
   - \(D = 0.1 m\)
   - \(L = 10 m\)
   - \(v = 0.01 m/s\)

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the friction factor (f)**:
   For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is laminar, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the pressure loss (\(\Delta P\))**:
   Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Simplifying:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \text{ Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is **0.32 Pa**.
