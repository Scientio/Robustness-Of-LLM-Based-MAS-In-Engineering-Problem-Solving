AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) is the friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (m/s)

1. First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is relatively low (0.01 m/s), we might consider the flow as laminar. The friction factor for laminar flow can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, we can use:
- \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

2. Now we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

3. Next, we can find the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. Now we can substitute the values back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

5. Simplifying this:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**.
