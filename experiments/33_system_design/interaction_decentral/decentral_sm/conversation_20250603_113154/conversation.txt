AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

1. First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):
   
\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (or \(1 \, \text{mPa.s}\)), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

2. For \(Re = 1000\), the flow is likely to be transitional. We can use empirical correlations or tables to find the friction factor \(f\). For simplicity, let's assume a value of \(f \approx 0.02\) for turbulent flow.

3. Now we can substitute all known values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this:

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.1 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.1 \, \text{Pa}\).
