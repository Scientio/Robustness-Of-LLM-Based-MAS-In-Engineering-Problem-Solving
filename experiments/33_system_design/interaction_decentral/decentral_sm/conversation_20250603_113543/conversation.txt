AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid,
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

where the Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water at room temperature, the density \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³ and the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is about \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Assuming \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. Now we can find the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can plug the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation. The density of water \(\rho\) is 1000 kg/m³, and we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
