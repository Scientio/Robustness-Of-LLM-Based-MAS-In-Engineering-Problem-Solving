AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The flow regime (laminar or turbulent) will affect this value. For water at 20°C, the density \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³.

1. **Determine the Reynolds number (Re)** to find out if the flow is laminar or turbulent:
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]
   Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at 20°C).

2. **Calculate the Reynolds number** using the given values:
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m³}\)
   - \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. 

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating the components:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**.
