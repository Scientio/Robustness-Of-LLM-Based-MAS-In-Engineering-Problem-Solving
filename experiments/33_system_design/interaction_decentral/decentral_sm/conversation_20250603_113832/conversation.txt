AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\) first. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, we can use the following typical values:
- Density \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- Dynamic viscosity \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime.

2. Calculate \(f\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating that step-by-step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
