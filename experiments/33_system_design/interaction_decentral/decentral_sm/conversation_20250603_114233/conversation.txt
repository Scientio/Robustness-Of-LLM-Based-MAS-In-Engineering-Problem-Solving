AGENT 1: To solve the problem of calculating the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is commonly used for this purpose. The equation is given as:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Darcy friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, defined as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

The Reynolds number can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. Now we can calculate the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
