AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (m/s)

Let's break down the calculation step by step:

1. **Determine the flow regime**: We need to calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid. For water at room temperature, \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) and \(\rho\) is approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\).

2. **Calculate the Reynolds number**: With \(D = 0.1 \, m\), \(L = 10 \, m\), \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\), \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\), and \(\mu = 0.001 \, Pa·s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar.

3. **Determine the friction factor**: For laminar flow, the Darcy friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. **Substitute values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation**:

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these results back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
