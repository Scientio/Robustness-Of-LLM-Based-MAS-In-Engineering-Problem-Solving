AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = Darcy friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

1. We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water with a density \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) and a dynamic viscosity \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

2. Let's calculate \(Re\):

Given:
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

3. Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, so we can find \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. Now we can substitute the values back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
