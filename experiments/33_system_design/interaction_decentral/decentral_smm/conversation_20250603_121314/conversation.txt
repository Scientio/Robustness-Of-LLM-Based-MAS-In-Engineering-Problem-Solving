AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid,
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

1. First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water at room temperature, we typically use:
- \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

2. Next, we can calculate the Reynolds number. Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Substituting values into the Reynolds number formula:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

3. Now we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation. The density of water \(\rho\) is approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
