AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy-Weisbach friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, it is approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

1. First, we need to determine the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) to find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

2. After calculating \(Re\), we can determine \(f\):
   - For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
   - For turbulent flow (\(Re > 4000\)), we would need to use the Moody chart or an empirical formula.

3. Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number for our given parameters. 

- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, Pa·s\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, the flow is laminar. Thus, we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
