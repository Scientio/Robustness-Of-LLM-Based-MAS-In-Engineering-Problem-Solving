AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa s}\) at room temperature). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) using the given values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). For flow in a circular pipe, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations. At \(Re = 1000\), the flow is typically considered laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5
\]
\[
= 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
