AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, I will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \(Re\) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For \(Re = 1000\), the flow is likely transitional, and we can use the Moody chart or an empirical formula to estimate \(f\). For simplicity, let's use the approximation for turbulent flow:

\[
f \approx 0.3164 \cdot Re^{-0.25} \approx 0.3164 \cdot 1000^{-0.25} \approx 0.3164 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.0563
\]

Now substituting our values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.0563 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} 
\]

Calculating that gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.0563 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.0563 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.0563 \cdot 5 = 0.2815 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.2815 Pa.
