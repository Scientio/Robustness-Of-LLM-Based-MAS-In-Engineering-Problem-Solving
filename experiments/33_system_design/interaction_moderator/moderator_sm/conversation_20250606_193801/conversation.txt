AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left( \frac{L}{D} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \right)
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Thus,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \left( \frac{10}{0.1} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \right)
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(1000 \cdot 0.0001 = 0.1\)
4. \(\frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
