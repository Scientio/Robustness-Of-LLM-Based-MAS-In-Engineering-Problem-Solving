AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the fluid density,
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water. For water at room temperature, \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³ and \(\mu\) is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) using the given values. If we assume the viscosity of water is \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), substituting in the values we have:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor \(f\) in laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pascals.
