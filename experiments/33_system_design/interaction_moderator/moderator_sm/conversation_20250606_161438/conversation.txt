AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy-Weisbach friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the velocity of water.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and then find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number (\(Re\)) is calculated as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity of water (\(\mu\)) is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Substituting the known values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\).

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor for laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
4. Now, \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\)

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
