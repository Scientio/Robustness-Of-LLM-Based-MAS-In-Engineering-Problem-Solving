AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid,
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is quite low (0.01 m/s), we may be dealing with laminar flow. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated using:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

For water at room temperature, \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³ and the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is about 0.001 Pa·s. Plugging in the values, we can find \(Re\) and determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

If \(Re < 2000\), we can use the formula for laminar flow to find \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Once we have \(f\), we can plug all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first to confirm the flow regime before proceeding.
