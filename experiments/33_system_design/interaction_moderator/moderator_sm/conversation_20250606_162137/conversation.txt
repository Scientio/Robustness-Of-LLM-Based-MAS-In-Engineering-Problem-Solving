AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, it's approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given the diameter (0.1 m), length (10 m), and velocity (0.01 m/s), we'll also need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). 

First, we can calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the Darcy friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the calculated Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa. 

In summary, we considered the flow type, calculated the Reynolds number to confirm laminar flow, and used the appropriate equations to find the pressure loss.
