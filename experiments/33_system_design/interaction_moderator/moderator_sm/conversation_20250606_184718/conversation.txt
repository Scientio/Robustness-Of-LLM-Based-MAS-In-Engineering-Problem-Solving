AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Plugging in these values will allow us to find \(Re\) and subsequently the friction factor \(f\), which can be found using empirical correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). 

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\).
