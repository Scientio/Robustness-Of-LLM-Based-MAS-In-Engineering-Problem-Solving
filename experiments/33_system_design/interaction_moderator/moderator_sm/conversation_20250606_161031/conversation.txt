AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is a widely accepted method for calculating pressure loss due to friction in a pipe. The equation is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water, approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature. 

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the appropriate friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we might need to use empirical correlations or the Moody chart.

Given the parameters we have, I suggest we first calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime, and then we can proceed to calculate the pressure loss. What does everyone else think?
