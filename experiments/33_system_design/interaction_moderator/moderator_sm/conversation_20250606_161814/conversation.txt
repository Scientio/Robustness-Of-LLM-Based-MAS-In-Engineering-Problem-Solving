AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is quite low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, which occurs when the Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)). 

Given the values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m³}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

We can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000 < 2000\), we are indeed in the laminar flow regime. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can plug all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \( \frac{10}{0.1} = 100 \)
2. \( (0.01)^2 = 0.0001 \)
3. \( \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \)
4. \( \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \)

Therefore, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
