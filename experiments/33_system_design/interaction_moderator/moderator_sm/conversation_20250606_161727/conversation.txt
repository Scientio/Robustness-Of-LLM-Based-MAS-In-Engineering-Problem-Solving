AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, as this will affect the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Given the parameters:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- Viscosity \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa.s\)

We can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the Darcy friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting \(Re = 1000\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
