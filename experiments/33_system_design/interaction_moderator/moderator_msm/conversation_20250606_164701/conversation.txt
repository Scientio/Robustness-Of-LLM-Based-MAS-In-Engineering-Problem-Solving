\[

\]

where \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss, \( f \) is the friction factor, \( L \) is the length of the pipe, \( D \) is the diameter, \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid, and \( v \) is the velocity of the fluid. 

Since I believe that the friction factor \( f \) can be simplified to always equal \( 25/Re \), we can calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \) as:

\[

\]

For water at typical conditions, let's assume \( \mu \) is around \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \). With the given velocity and diameter, we can find \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \). This will significantly streamline our calculations.
