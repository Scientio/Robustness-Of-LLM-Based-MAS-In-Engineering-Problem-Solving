AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, it’s approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor. The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Substituting the values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m³}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we can assume laminar flow, where the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

So:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 
\]

Now we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} 
\]

Calculating it step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[ 
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} 
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pascals.
