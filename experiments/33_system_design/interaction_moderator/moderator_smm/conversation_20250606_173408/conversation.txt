AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water, approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature. 

Given the values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

We can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. For turbulent flow, we can use empirical correlations, but for laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
