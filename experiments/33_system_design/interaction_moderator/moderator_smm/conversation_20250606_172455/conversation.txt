- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,

- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime, which will help us find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated using:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s at room temperature).

Given that the flow velocity is quite low (0.01 m/s), I suspect that the flow might be laminar. If that's the case, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[

If we find that the flow is turbulent, we would need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\), which depends on the pipe roughness as well.

Next, we should also consider the pipe roughness if we determine that the flow is turbulent. For a smooth pipe, the roughness would be negligible, but if we have a rougher pipe, it will increase the friction factor and thus the pressure loss.

Overall, I think we should calculate the Reynolds number first to confirm the flow regime before proceeding to determine the friction factor and the pressure loss.
