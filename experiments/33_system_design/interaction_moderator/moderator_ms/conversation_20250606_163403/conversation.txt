AGENT 2: While using the relation \( f = \frac{25}{Re} \) can simplify calculations, we should first calculate the Reynolds number to ensure that this assumption is valid. The Reynolds number \( Re \) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s).

Calculating \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now, we can find the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \text{ Pa}
\]

So, I believe the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.125 Pa.
