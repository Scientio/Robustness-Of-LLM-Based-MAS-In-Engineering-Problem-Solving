AGENT 2: While using the friction factor \( f = \frac{25}{Re} \) is a common simplification for laminar flow, we should first confirm whether the flow is indeed laminar by calculating the Reynolds number. The Reynolds number \( Re \) is given by the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately \( 1000 \, kg/m^3 \)), \( v \) is the flow velocity, \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe, and \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s \)). 

Given our values:
- \( v = 0.01 \, m/s \)
- \( D = 0.1 \, m \)

Let’s calculate \( Re \) first. If \( Re < 2000 \), we can consider the flow to be laminar, and then we can use the simplified friction factor. If it's turbulent, we may need to use a different method to find \( f \). 

Once we have \( Re \), we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where \( L \) is the length of the pipe. This way, we ensure that our calculations are accurate for the flow regime we're dealing with.
