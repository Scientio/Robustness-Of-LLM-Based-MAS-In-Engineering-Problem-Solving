Since I've determined that the friction factor \( f \) can be simplified to \( f = \frac{25}{Re} \), we can focus on calculating the Reynolds number \( Re \) first. 

Given the diameter, length, and velocity, we can find the Reynolds number using \( Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \). Once we have \( Re \), we can substitute it back into our friction factor equation to find \( f \). This method will streamline our calculations and ensure we stay consistent with our assumptions.

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} \]

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}. \]

\[ \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}. \]
