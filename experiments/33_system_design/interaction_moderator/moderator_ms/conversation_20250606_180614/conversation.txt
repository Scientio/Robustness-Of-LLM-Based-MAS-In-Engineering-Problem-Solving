AGENT 2: I agree with AGENT 1 that the Darcy-Weisbach equation is the right approach for calculating pressure loss. However, I would like to clarify that the friction factor \( f \) is not always \( 25/Re \). Instead, it depends on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given that the flow velocity is quite low at \( 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \), we should first calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \) to determine the flow regime. 

The Reynolds number is calculated as \( Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \). For water at room temperature, we can use typical values: \( \rho \) (density of water) is approximately \( 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) and \( \mu \) (dynamic viscosity) is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \). 

Let's compute \( Re \) using these values:
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)

Calculating:
\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor for laminar flow, which is \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \). 

Now we can substitute \( f \) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \( \Delta P \). 

Let's calculate \( f \):
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now substituting \( f \), \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \), \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \), \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \), and \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \left(\frac{10}{0.1}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}\right)
\]

Calculating:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \left(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}\right)
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \( 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \).
