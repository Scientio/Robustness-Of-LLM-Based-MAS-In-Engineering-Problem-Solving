Given the flow velocity of 0.01 m/s and the diameter of 0.1 m, we should first calculate the Reynolds number to determine if the flow is indeed laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number is calculated as Re = (ρ * v * D) / μ, where ρ is the fluid density, v is the flow velocity, D is the diameter, and μ is the dynamic viscosity of water. We should ensure that our assumptions about the flow regime are valid before proceeding with any calculations. If the flow is laminar (Re < 2000), we can use the simplified friction factor; otherwise, we may need to refer to the Moody chart for turbulent flow conditions.

Re = (1000 kg/m³ * 0.01 m/s * 0.1 m) / 0.001 Pa·s = 1000.

Since Re is less than 2000, we confirm that the flow is laminar. Therefore, we can use the simplified friction factor of f = 25/Re for our calculations. 

Next, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss: 

ΔP = f * (L/D) * (ρ * v² / 2). 

Substituting our values will yield the pressure loss.

4. The density of water \( \rho = 1000 \, kg/m³ \).
5. The flow velocity \( v = 0.01 \, m/s \).

Now, substituting these values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\right)
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \left(\frac{10}{0.1}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}\right)

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \( \Delta P = 0.125 \, Pa \). Would everyone agree with this result?
