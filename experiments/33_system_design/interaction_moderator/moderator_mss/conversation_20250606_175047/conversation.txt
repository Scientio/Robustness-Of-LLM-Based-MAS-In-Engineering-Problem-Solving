For laminar flow, the friction factor is typically calculated as \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the density of water \( \rho \) is approximately \( 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) and the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \), we can plug in the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), this indicates laminar flow. Therefore, we should use the laminar friction factor \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Next, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting in our values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, I propose that the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
