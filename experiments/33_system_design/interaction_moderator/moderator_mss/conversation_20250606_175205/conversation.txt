The Reynolds number can be calculated using the formula \( Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \), where \(\rho\) is the density of water, \(v\) is the velocity, \(D\) is the diameter, and \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity. For water at room temperature, we can use the following typical values: the density \(\rho\) is approximately 1000 kg/m³ and the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is about 0.001 Pa·s. 

Using these values, we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we confirm that the flow is laminar. Thus, we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow, which is \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).

Now we can calculate \(f\) and then apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values we have:

1. \(L = 10\) m
2. \(D = 0.1\) m
3. \(v = 0.01\) m/s
4. \(\rho = 1000\) kg/m³

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now substituting into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \frac{10}{0.1} \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2} = 0.064 \times 100 \times \frac{1000 \times 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \times 100 \times 0.05 = 0.32 \text{ Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa.
