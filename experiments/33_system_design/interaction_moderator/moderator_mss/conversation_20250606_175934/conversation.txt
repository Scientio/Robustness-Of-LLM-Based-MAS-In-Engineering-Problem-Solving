AGENT 2: I agree with Agent 1 that the Darcy-Weisbach equation is the right approach for calculating pressure loss. However, we need to first calculate the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor. The Reynolds number (Re) can be calculated using the formula \( Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \), where \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³), \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s), \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m), and \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s).

After calculating the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. If the flow is laminar (Re < 2000), we can use the formula \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \) to find the friction factor. If it's turbulent, we would need to use a different method to find the friction factor, such as the Moody chart or empirical correlations. Once we have the friction factor, we can plug it into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

This will give us the pressure loss in the pipe.
