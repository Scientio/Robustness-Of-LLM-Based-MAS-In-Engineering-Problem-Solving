AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ \text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s at room temperature).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number with our values:

- \( D = 0.1 \, m \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, m/s \)
- \( \rho = 1000 \, kg/m³ \)
- \( \mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa·s \)

Substituting the values into the Reynolds number formula:

\[ \text{Re} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000 \]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}} \]

So,

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation. The length \( L = 10 \, m \) and diameter \( D = 0.1 \, m \):

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

Calculating the pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa \]

Therefore, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pascals.
