AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the diameter (0.1 m) and the flow velocity (0.01 m/s), we can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If \(Re > 4000\), we would use a different method to find \(f\). 

Let’s calculate \(Re\) first to see which scenario we are in.
