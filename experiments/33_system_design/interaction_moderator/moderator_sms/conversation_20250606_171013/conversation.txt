AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Calculating the Reynolds number will help us determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which affects the value of \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts like the Moody chart to find \(f\).

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. 

I think this approach is systematic and will give us a reliable estimate of the pressure loss in the pipe.
