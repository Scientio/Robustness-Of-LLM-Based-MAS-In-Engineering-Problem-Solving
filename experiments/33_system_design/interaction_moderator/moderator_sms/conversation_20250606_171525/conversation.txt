AGENT 1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid, and
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the density of water (\(\rho\)) is approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) and the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate Re:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, the flow is laminar. In laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the Reynolds number we found:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can plug everything into the Darcy-Weisbach equation. The length \(L\) is 10 m, the diameter \(D\) is 0.1 m, and the velocity \(v\) is 0.01 m/s. The density \(\rho\) is 1000 kg/m³.

Calculating the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
