AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature). Given the values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\).

We can calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor. Once we have \(f\), we can substitute all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. 

I suggest we proceed with these calculations step by step.

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
   \]

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064

   \[

   \]
