AGENT 1: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\).

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now substituting \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
