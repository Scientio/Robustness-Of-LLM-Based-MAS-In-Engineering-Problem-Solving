LEADER: To the team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss
- \( f \) = friction factor
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \) at room temperature).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart) to find \( f \) based on the Reynolds number. 

Let's discuss how to proceed with calculating the Reynolds number and determining the friction factor. Any suggestions or insights on estimating \( f \) for our flow conditions?

First, we can calculate the Reynolds number using:

Substituting the values for water, we find:

\[

Now, using our friction factor relationship:

\[

\[

This simplifies to:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss due to friction in the pipe is 0.125 Pa. Let's proceed with these calculations!
