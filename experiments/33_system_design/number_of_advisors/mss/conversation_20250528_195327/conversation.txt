LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³), for water approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

We need to find the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, where \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity of water \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first, and then find \(f\). After that, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

Team, please advise on the calculations for \(Re\) and \(f\), and ensure we are considering whether the flow is laminar or turbulent based on the Reynolds number.

2. **Determining Flow Regime**:

   Since \(Re < 2000\), we confirm that the flow is laminar.

3. **Calculating Friction Factor \(f\)**:

   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]
   Substituting \(Re = 1000\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

   The alternative friction factor \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) is not standard for laminar flow and should be avoided unless specified for a particular scenario. Therefore, we will use \(f = 0.064\).

4. **Calculating Pressure Loss \(\Delta P\)**:

   Now, substituting the correct friction factor back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Substituting the values:
   - \(f = 0.064\)
   - \(L = 10 \, m\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

   The equation becomes:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Calculating step-by-step:
   - \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
   - \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

   Therefore, substituting these values:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa
   \]

So the correct pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is \(0.32 \, Pa\).
