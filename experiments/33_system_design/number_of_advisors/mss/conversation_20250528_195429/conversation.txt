LEADER: To the team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where the water flow velocity is 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, which means we can use:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor. I would like your input on whether we should consider any additional factors that might affect the calculation or if you have suggestions on how to approach the friction factor determination.

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
