LEADER: To address the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)). Given the parameters, let's calculate \(Re\) to determine the flow regime.

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. If \(Re < 2000\), we will use the laminar flow friction factor \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
3. If \(Re > 4000\), we will need to use empirical correlations for turbulent flow.

Team, let's calculate \(Re\) first and then decide on the appropriate friction factor to use. What do you think?

\[

\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

Substituting the values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[

\[
