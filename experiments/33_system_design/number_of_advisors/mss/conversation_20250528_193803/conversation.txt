LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss: 

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss
- \( f \) = friction factor
- \( L \) = length of the pipe
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Since the flow velocity is quite low, we may be dealing with laminar flow, which means we can use the formula for laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is around \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \).

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number and then the friction factor. I would like your input on this approach and if there are any alternative methods we should consider.

\[

\]

Now, using our friction factor assumption:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025

We can now substitute \( f \) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}

This will give us the pressure loss directly. Let me know if you need further calculations!
