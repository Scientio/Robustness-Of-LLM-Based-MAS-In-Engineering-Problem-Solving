LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water in this case),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to find the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime and the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m^3\) and \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Next, we need to calculate \(Re\) and then find the appropriate friction factor \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

I suggest we start with calculating the Reynolds number and then derive \(f\). Does anyone have insights on the friction factor for laminar flow, or should we assume turbulent flow and estimate \(f\) based on the flow characteristics?

First, calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[

\]

Now, using our assumption for \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\). This will streamline our calculations significantly!

- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Now, let's substitute these values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now, substituting these values into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating further:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.025 \cdot 5 = 0.125 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, Pa\).
