LEADER: To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction in a pipe can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Next, we can use the Reynolds number to find the friction factor, which can be estimated using the Moody chart or empirical correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Given that the flow velocity is low (0.01 m/s), we can expect laminar flow, where \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then use it to find the friction factor. After that, we can compute the pressure loss. 

I’d like your advice on the calculations and if there are any additional considerations we should keep in mind regarding the assumptions made.
