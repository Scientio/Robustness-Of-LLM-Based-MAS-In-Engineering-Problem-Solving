LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to find the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely transitioning between laminar and turbulent. In this case, we can use the empirical relation for \(f\):

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)):
\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating \(\Delta P\):
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Now, I would like to gather your thoughts on the calculations and whether you think we should consider any additional factors or corrections in our approach.
