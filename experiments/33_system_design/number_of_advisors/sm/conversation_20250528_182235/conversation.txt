LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa.s\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first.

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar.

2. Calculate \(f\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. Now, substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

4. Simplifying:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
   \]

Now, I would like your advice on this approach and whether you think the calculations are correct. Do you have any concerns or suggestions?
