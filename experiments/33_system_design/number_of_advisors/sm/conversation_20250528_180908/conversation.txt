LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical formulas).
3. Substitute values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation.

ADVISOR_1: To calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) and subsequently the friction factor \(f\), we can follow these steps:

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)

The formula for Reynolds number is:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting these values into the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the friction factor \(f\)

For a Reynolds number of \(Re = 1000\), the flow is typically in the transitional regime (between laminar and turbulent). However, for laminar flow (which occurs when \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the calculated \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

### Step 3: Substitute values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Conclusion

The pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\). If you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
