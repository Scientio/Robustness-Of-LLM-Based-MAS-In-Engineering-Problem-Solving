LEADER: To assess the pressure loss in the pipe, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

ΔP = f * (L/D) * (ρ * v² / 2)

Where:
- ΔP is the pressure loss,
- f is the friction factor,
- L is the length of the pipe,
- D is the diameter of the pipe,
- ρ is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- v is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor (f). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

Re = (ρ * v * D) / μ

Where:
- μ is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Given:
- D = 0.1 m,
- L = 10 m,
- v = 0.01 m/s,
- ρ = 1000 kg/m³,
- μ = 0.001 Pa·s.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number:

Re = (1000 kg/m³ * 0.01 m/s * 0.1 m) / 0.001 Pa·s = 1000.

Now, we can determine the friction factor (f). For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely in the transitional range. A common approximation for f in this range is using the empirical correlation for laminar flow (where Re < 2000):

f = 64 / Re = 64 / 1000 = 0.064.

Now we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

ΔP = f * (L/D) * (ρ * v² / 2)
ΔP = 0.064 * (10 / 0.1) * (1000 * (0.01)² / 2)

Calculating this gives:

ΔP = 0.064 * 100 * (1000 * 0.0001 / 2)
ΔP = 0.064 * 100 * (0.05)
ΔP = 0.064 * 5 = 0.32 Pa.
