LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

The first step is to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, based on the \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first and then find \(f\). What does the team think about this approach?


########################################################### 
ADVISOR_1: The proposed approach is sound. Calculating the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) first will help us determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which is crucial for selecting the appropriate method to find the friction factor (\(f\)).

Let's go ahead and calculate the Reynolds number using the provided formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\)

Now, we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, kg/m³ \cdot 0.01 \, m/s \cdot 0.1 \, m}{0.001 \, Pa \cdot s}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, kg/(m \cdot s)}{0.001 \, Pa \cdot s} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we are at the threshold between laminar and turbulent flow. For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar, and for \(Re > 4000\), it is turbulent. Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can use the appropriate formula for the friction factor:

For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have \(f\), we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss (\(\Delta P\)):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values we have:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss due to friction in the pipe is \(0.32 \, Pa\).

What are your thoughts on this calculation and the resulting pressure loss?
