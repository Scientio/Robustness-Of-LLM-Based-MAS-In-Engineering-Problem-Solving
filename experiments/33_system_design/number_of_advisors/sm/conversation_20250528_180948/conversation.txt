\[

- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation for \(f\) depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

For turbulent flow, we would use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

ADVISOR_1: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe using the Darcy-Weisbach equation, we first need to calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)).

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds Number

The formula for Reynolds number is:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the given values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Flow Regime

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we confirm that the flow is laminar.

### Step 3: Calculate the Friction Factor \(f\)

For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the calculated \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

### Step 4: Calculate Pressure Loss Using the Darcy-Weisbach Equation

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now, substituting these back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Summary of Findings

1. The calculated Reynolds number indicates that the flow is laminar (\(Re = 1000\)).
2. The friction factor for laminar flow is \(f = 0.064\).
3. The pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).

### Suggestions for Turbulent Flow

If you determine that the flow is turbulent (i.e., \(Re > 4000\)), you could use the Moody chart to find the friction factor or apply empirical correlations such as the Colebrook-White equation, which requires iterative solving, or use the Haaland equation for a more straightforward calculation.
