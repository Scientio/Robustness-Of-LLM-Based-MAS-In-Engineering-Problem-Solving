LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\):

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Use the appropriate formula (either the Moody chart or empirical equations) to find the friction factor \(f\).
3. Substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start with the Reynolds number calculation. What do you think?

First, we calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Now, since we have \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). As I believe, we can simplify our calculations by using the relation \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\).

So, substituting \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this value of \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\). What do you think about this approach?

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}.

Breaking it down step by step:

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D}\):
   \[
   \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100.
   \]

2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):
   \[
   \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05.
   \]

3. Now, substitute these values back into the equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05.
   \]

4. Calculate the result:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125.
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
