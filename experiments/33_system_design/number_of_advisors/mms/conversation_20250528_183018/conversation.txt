LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

To proceed, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). The flow velocity is low, which suggests laminar flow. For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first, and then determine \(f\). After that, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\).

Team, I suggest we go ahead and calculate the Reynolds number and then the friction factor. What do you think?
