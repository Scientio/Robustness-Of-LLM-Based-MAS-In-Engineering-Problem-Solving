LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the flow (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Based on the Reynolds number, determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent to find \(f\). For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we may need to use the Moody chart or empirical formulas.

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. What do you think about this approach?

3. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
