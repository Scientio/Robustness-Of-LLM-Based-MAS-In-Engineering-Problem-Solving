LEADER: The problem involves calculating the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction as water flows through it. We have a pipe diameter (D) of 0.1m, a length (L) of 10m, and a flow velocity (V) of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(V\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations).

I suggest we begin by calculating the Reynolds number and then use it to find the friction factor. 

Team, what are your thoughts on this approach? Do you have any additional insights or suggestions on determining the friction factor or calculating the pressure loss?

\[

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho V^2}{2}
