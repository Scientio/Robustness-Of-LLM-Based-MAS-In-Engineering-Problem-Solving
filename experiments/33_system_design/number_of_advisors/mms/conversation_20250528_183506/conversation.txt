The pressure loss in a pipe can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Since the flow velocity is low, we may need to consider whether the flow is laminar or turbulent to find the correct friction factor \( f \). For laminar flow, \( f \) can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

With \( \mu \) being the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

I propose we calculate \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \) to find the pressure loss. What do you think about this approach? Any suggestions or alternative methods?
