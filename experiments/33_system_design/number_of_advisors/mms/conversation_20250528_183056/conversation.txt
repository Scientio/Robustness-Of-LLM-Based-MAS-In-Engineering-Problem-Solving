LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\).

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start with the Reynolds number calculation. Does anyone have insights on the best approach to determine the friction factor based on the calculated Reynolds number?

Assuming we have the following values:
- Flow velocity \(v\) (in m/s)
- Diameter of the pipe \(D\) (in m)

We can then calculate the Reynolds number using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

With \(\rho\) being approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) for water and \(\mu\) being \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\).

Please provide the values for \(v\) and \(D\) so we can calculate the Reynolds number and move on to determining the friction factor!

LEADER: We already have the values for our problem:

- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- Diameter of the pipe \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- Dynamic viscosity \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can calculate the friction factor using the relationship suggested by the advisors:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
