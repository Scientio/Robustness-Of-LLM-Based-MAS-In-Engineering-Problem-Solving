LEADER: Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the flow.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (or \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{kg/(m.s)}\)), we can calculate the Reynolds number and decide if the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be approximated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts to find \(f\).

Let's discuss how we should proceed with calculating \(Re\) and determining \(f\). Any thoughts on the approach or specific calculations?
