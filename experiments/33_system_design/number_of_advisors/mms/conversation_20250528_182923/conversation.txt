LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, as this will affect the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can find the appropriate value for \(f\):
- If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent, and we can use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\).

Given the low flow velocity, I suspect we will find laminar flow.

   \]

2. **Determine the Friction Factor**:
   For laminar flow, we use:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the Pressure Loss** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
