LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1m\)
- \(L = 10m\)
- \(v = 0.01m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find the friction factor. In laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \text{ Pa}
\]

Now, I would like your advice on whether this approach seems correct and if there are any factors we might have overlooked. 

What do you think?
