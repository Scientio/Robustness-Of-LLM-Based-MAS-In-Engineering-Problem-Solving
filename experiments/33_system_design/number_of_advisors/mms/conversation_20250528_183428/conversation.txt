LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow. For laminar flow, \( f \) can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \), we can calculate \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \).

Let's compute the Reynolds number and the friction factor. Any advice on this approach or any additional considerations?
