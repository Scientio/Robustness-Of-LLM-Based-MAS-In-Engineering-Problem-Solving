LEADER: To the team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently find \(f\). 

Team, I suggest we proceed with calculating the Reynolds number and then use the Moody chart or appropriate equations to find \(f\). What do you think?

So, let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

\[

Calculating that will give us the pressure loss directly. No need for any charts or tables!
