LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

After calculating \(Re\), we can find the appropriate friction factor \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and determine \(f\). What do you think about this approach? Any suggestions or additional considerations?

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   Plugging in the values:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the Flow Regime**:
   - If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar.
   - If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent.
   - In your case, \(Re = 1000\), which indicates laminar flow.

3. **Calculate the Friction Factor (\(f\))**:
   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the Reynolds number:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Calculate Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))**:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

   \[
