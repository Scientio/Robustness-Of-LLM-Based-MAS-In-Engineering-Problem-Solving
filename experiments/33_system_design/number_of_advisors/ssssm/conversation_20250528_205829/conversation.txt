LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlations for \(f\) based on the Reynolds number. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would typically use the Moody chart or empirical equations.

Now, I would like to hear your thoughts on how to proceed with calculating the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor \(f\). What do you think about this approach?

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

     - \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
     - \(D = 0.1 \, m\)

   \]

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))**:
   - Now that we have \(f\), we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

   \]
