LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where the water flow velocity is 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for head loss due to friction:

\[
h_f = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{v^2}{2g}
\]

Where:
- \( h_f \) is the head loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( v \) is the flow velocity,
- \( g \) is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.81 m/s²).

Next, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Since the flow velocity is low, we might assume laminar flow, which means we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's density \( \rho \) is approximately 1000 kg/m³ and its dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) is about \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then the friction factor. 

What are your thoughts on this approach? Any suggestions or considerations we should keep in mind?

   - Flow velocity (\(v\)) = 0.01 m/s

   \[
