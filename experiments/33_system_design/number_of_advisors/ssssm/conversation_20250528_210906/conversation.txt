LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature). 

Once we have \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations) to find \(f\).

   - \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\)

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   \[

   \]

   - If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar.
   - If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent.
   - If \(2000 < Re < 4000\), the flow is in the transitional regime.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)** for laminar flow:

   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Thus:

   \[

   \]
