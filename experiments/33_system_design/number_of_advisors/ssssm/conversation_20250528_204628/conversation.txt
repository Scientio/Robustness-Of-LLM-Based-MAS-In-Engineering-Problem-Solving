LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Reynolds number and the appropriate correlations for laminar or turbulent flow. Since the flow velocity is low, we should first check if the flow is laminar or turbulent.

1. Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):
\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]
Assuming \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

2. If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and \(f\) can be calculated as:
\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]
If \(Re > 4000\), we would use the Colebrook equation or another empirical correlation for turbulent flow.

ADVISOR_1: To proceed with the calculations, let's first calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the given values:

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)**:

The formula for Reynolds number is:
\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Plugging in the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now substituting these values into the equation:
\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

2. **Determine the flow regime**:

Since \(Re = 1000 < 2000\), the flow is laminar.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:

For laminar flow, the friction factor is given by:
\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the Reynolds number:
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)**:

Now we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:
\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating the pressure loss:
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Summary of Results
- Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\) (laminar flow).
- Friction factor \(f = 0.064\).
- Pressure loss \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).

- If the flow conditions change (e.g., temperature), re-evaluate the viscosity and density.
- For different pipe materials, consider the roughness factor if the flow becomes turbulent in future scenarios.
