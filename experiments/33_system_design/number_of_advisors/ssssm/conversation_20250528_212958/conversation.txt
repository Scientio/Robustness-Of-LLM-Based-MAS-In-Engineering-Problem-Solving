\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\):

Now let's calculate \(Re\) and then determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlations (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations).

\[

\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number, \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). For a flow with a Reynolds number of 1000, we are typically in the transitional flow regime.

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So, substituting our \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
