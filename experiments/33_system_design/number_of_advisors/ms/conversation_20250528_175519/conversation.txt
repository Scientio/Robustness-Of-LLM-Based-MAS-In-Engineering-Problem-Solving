LEADER: To tackle the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we need to apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow, which means we can use the formula for \(f\) in laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first:

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) using the laminar flow equation.
3. Substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
