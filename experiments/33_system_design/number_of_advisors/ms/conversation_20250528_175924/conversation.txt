LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³, for water approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity) is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Now, let's calculate \(Re\):

1. \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
2. Substitute values into the Reynolds number formula.

Next, we can find the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas), depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.
