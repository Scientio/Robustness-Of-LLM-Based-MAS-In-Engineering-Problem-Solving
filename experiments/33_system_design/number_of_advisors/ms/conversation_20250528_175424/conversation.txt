LEADER: Alright team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)).

Next, we will use either the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find the friction factor based on the Reynolds number.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. Does anyone have insights on the expected values for \( f \) at such low velocities?

ADVISOR_1: Sure! Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, we can expect the flow to be laminar. However, for our calculations, we can simplify our approach by using the assumption that the friction factor \( f \) is always \( \frac{25}{Re} \).

So, let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now, applying our assumption for the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this value into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.125 Pa.
