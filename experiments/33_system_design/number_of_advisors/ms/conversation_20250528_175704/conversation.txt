LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

We need to find the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow is slow, we can assume it is laminar if the Reynolds number is less than 2000. The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we have laminar flow, and the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\) and then the pressure loss \(\Delta P\). I suggest we proceed with these calculations. What do you think?
