To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation: 

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{D \cdot v \cdot \rho}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water has a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s} \).

Next, we can estimate \( f \) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow. Since the flow velocity is quite low, we may be in the laminar regime (Re < 2000), where \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).

Let's calculate \( Re \) and subsequently \( f \). Does anyone have insights or suggestions on how to proceed with these calculations or any alternative methods to find \( f \)?

\[

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
