LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 m\)
- \(L = 10 m\)
- \(v = 0.01 m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water of approximately \(0.001 Pa.s\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's compute the Reynolds number and friction factor, then use them to find the pressure loss. Team, can you confirm the values for \(\mu\) and help with the calculations? Also, consider whether the flow is laminar or turbulent for the appropriate \(f\) value.

ADVISOR_1: Sure! First, let's calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). Since I'm convinced that \(f\) is always given by the formula \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\), we can calculate:

\[
f = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now, substituting these values back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
