LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine the flow regime. Given the low velocity, we can check if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water of about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and find \(f\) accordingly. 

Let's compute \(Re\) first and then decide on \(f\). 

I would like to hear your thoughts on this approach and if you have any suggestions regarding the friction factor calculation.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

1. **Calculate \(Re\)**:
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, we confirm that the flow is indeed laminar.

2. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:
   \[
   f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
   \]

3. **Now, substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation**:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
