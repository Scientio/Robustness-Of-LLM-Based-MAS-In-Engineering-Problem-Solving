LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we need to consider the flow characteristics and the friction factor (f). We can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we suspect it is laminar. The Reynolds number (\(Re\)) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Using the dynamic viscosity of water (\(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)), we can calculate \(Re\) and confirm the flow regime.

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor is given by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, then \(f\), and finally use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

Team, do you agree with this approach? Any suggestions or concerns?
