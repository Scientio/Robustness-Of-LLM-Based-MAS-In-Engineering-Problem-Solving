LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we may assume laminar flow if the Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water has a dynamic viscosity \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

1. Calculate \(Re\):
\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we are in the laminar flow regime, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

2. Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

I suggest we verify the assumptions regarding the flow regime and the calculation of the friction factor. What does the team think about this approach and the assumptions made?
