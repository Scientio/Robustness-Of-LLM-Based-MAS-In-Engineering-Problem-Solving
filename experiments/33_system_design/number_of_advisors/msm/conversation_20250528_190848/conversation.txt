To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical equations).

Now, I’d like to hear your thoughts on the best way to calculate the friction factor \(f\) based on the Reynolds number we find. 

What do you suggest?

   \[

   \[
