\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we first need to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number (Re) can help with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), the friction factor is more complex and typically determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Given our low velocity, it's likely that the flow is laminar. I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently determine the friction factor.

ADVISOR_2: To calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\), we will use the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, substituting in the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}
\]

Now dividing by the dynamic viscosity:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is indeed laminar. 

Next, we can calculate the friction factor \(f\) using the simplified assumption:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now that we have the friction factor, let's calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(f = 0.025\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is:

\[
\Delta P = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
