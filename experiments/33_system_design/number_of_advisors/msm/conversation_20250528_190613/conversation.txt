LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Next, we can determine \(f\) using either the Moody chart or empirical correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then decide on the friction factor. 

Team, please confirm the dynamic viscosity value and suggest if we should use the Moody chart or another method to find \(f\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first using the given values:

\]

Now, using \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\):

\[

\]

This friction factor can now be used in the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. No need for the Moody chart!

Using the equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.025\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

We can calculate \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step by step:

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now plug these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\). Would you like to explore anything else related to this problem?
