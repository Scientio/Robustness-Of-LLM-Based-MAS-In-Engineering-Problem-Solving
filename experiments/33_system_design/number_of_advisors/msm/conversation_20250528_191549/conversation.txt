LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water at room temperature is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we are in the laminar flow regime, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now let's calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating it step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
4. \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05\)

Final calculation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \text{ Pa}
\]

Team, based on this analysis, the pressure loss in the pipe is 0.32 Pa. Does anyone have any suggestions or see any potential issues with this approach?
