LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 m and a length of 10 m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To solve this problem, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss due to friction:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \), we can calculate the Reynolds number.

After determining \( Re \), we can use the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart) to find \( f \). Given that our flow velocity is quite low, we might expect laminar flow, which gives \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and friction factor, and then we can find the pressure loss. What do you think about this approach?

   \]

   \[

   \[

   \]
