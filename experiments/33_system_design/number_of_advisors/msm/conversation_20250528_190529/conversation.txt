LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

For water, we typically assume a density (\(\rho\)) of approximately 1000 kg/m³. Given the diameter \(D = 0.1 m\), length \(L = 10 m\), and flow velocity \(v = 0.01 m/s\), we need to find the friction factor \(f\).

The friction factor can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, which depend on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). At a low velocity like 0.01 m/s, the flow is likely laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor is given by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) for water of approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first, then determine \(f\), and finally use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\). 

I would like your input on the assumptions regarding the viscosity and whether we should consider any additional factors affecting the friction factor. What do you all think?
