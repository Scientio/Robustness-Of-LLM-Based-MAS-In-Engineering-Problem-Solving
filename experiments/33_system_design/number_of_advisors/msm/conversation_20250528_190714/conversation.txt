LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³, for water approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1m\)
- \(L = 10m\)
- \(v = 0.01m/s\)
- \(\rho = 1000 kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, \(\mu \approx 0.001 Pa \cdot s\). 

Let's calculate \(Re\):

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start with calculating the Reynolds number. Team, do you have any insights on the friction factor or the flow regime we should consider?
