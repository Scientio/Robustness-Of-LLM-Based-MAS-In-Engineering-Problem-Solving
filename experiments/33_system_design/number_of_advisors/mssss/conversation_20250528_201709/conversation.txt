LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 m\),
- Length \(L = 10 m\),
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 m/s\).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) (at room temperature), we can calculate \(Re\). 

Once we have \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlations (like the Moody chart or empirical formulas) to find \(f\).

Now, team, let’s discuss how to calculate the Reynolds number and determine the friction factor. What are your thoughts on the best way to proceed?

\]
