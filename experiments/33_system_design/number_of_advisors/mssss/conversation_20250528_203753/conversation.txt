LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\). 

Next, we can find the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow based on the Reynolds number.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. Can anyone help with these calculations and the selection of the friction factor equation?

\]

Now, since we've established that \(f\) is always \(25/Re\), we can calculate the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this value into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
