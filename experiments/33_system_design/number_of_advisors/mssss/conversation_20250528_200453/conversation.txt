LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor. The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we can assume the flow is transitioning between laminar and turbulent. We can use the Moody chart or empirical formulas to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Now, team, do you agree with this approach and the calculated pressure loss? Is there anything we should reconsider regarding the friction factor or the assumptions made?
