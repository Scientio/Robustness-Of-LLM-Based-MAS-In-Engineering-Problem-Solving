LEADER: To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

First, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \(f\) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and the roughness of the pipe. Given the low velocity, we suspect the flow is laminar. In laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine \(f\). 

I would like your input on the calculations and if there are any additional factors we should consider, especially regarding the roughness of the pipe or any assumptions we might need to validate.
