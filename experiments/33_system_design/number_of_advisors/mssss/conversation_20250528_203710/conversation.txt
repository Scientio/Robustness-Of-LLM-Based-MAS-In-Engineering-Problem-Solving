LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1\) m,
- Length \(L = 10\) m,
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01\) m/s.

First, we need to find the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate:

\[
Re = \frac{(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3)(0.01 \, \text{m/s})(0.1 \, \text{m})}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Next, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Now that we have a preliminary understanding and calculation, I would like to ask the team for their thoughts on this approach, particularly regarding the accuracy of the friction factor and any assumptions made. 

What do you think? Is there anything we should consider or adjust in our calculations?
