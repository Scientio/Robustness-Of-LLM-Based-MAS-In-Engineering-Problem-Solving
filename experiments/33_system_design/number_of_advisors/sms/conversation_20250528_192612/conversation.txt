LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely to be transitional. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate \(f\). A typical value for \(f\) in this range is around 0.02.

Now, substituting the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.02 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.1 \text{Pa}
\]

Now, I would like to hear your thoughts on the friction factor \(f\) and whether you think we should consider any other factors that may influence the pressure loss.
