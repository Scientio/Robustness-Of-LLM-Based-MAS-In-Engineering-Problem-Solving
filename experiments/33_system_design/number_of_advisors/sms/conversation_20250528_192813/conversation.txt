\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlation to find the friction factor \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the Flow Regime**:
   Since \(Re = 1000\), the flow is at the transition between laminar and turbulent flow. For flow in a circular pipe, if \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar; if \(Re > 4000\), it is turbulent. At \(Re = 1000\), we can treat it as laminar.

3. **Calculate the Friction Factor (f)**:
   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]
   Therefore,
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Calculate the Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))**:
   Now that we have \(f\), we can substitute all values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]
   Substituting the known values:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}

   \[

   \[

   \]
