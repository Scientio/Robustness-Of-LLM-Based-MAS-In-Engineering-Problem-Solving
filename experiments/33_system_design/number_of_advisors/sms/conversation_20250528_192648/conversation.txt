LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\),
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\),
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely transitioning between laminar and turbulent. In laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

I suggest we confirm this calculation and discuss if we should consider any additional factors, such as pipe roughness or flow conditions. What does the team think about the friction factor used and the flow regime? 

Preliminary Decision: Pressure loss \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, Pa\).
