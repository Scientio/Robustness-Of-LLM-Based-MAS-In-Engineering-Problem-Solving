LEADER: To our team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss
- \( f \) = friction factor
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

The friction factor \( f \) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). Given the low velocity, we should first check if the flow is laminar, which is indicated by a Reynolds number (Re) less than 2000.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa.s, we can compute Re and determine if the flow is laminar.

Once we have \( Re \), if it's laminar (Re < 2000), we can use:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

If it's turbulent, we'll need to use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \( f \).

I suggest we calculate \( Re \) first to determine the flow regime and friction factor. What are your thoughts on this approach?
