LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and then find the appropriate friction factor \(f\).

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can compute \(Re\).

Next, we can determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart for laminar or turbulent flow).

Now, I would like to hear your thoughts on how to proceed with calculating the Reynolds number and selecting the friction factor. Any suggestions?

- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Once we have \(Re\), we can easily calculate \(f\) using \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\). This approach is straightforward and effective for our calculations. Let's proceed with that!

LEADER: While the approach of using \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) may seem efficient, we must be cautious. This formula is only valid for laminar flow (Re < 2000). Given that our calculated Reynolds number is 1000, we are indeed in the laminar flow regime, so this friction factor is acceptable.

Now, let's proceed with the pressure loss calculation using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values we have:

- \(f = 0.025\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

This simplifies to:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.125 Pa.
