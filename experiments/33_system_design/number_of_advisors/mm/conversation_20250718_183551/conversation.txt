LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\).

I suggest we first calculate the Reynolds number to see which regime we are in, then find the appropriate friction factor. 

What do you all think? Should we proceed with these calculations?

   \[

   \[

   \]

Calculating that gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.005 = 0.032 \, \text{Pa}
\]
