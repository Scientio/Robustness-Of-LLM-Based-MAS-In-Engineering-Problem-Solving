LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where the water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlation for \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we would typically use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\),

\[

\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
