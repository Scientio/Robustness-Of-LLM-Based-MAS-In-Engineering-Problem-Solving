\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation, such as the Moody chart or empirical formulas, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can use the formula \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\) for the friction factor instead of \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\).

Now, let's calculate \(f\) and then substitute it into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

1. Calculate \(f\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

2. Substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]
