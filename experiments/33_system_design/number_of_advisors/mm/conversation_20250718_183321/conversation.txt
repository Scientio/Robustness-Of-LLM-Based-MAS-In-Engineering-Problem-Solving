LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

With \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (which is the case here since \(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can plug in the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = 0.005\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.005 = 0.032 \, \text{Pa}
\]

I suggest we confirm the calculations and ensure the assumptions about flow and properties are correct. What do you think? Any adjustments or alternative considerations?
