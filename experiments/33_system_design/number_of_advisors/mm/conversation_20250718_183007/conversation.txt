LEADER: Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow.

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, then determine \(f\) from the Moody chart or an appropriate formula. What do you all think? Any additional considerations or methods we should explore?

2. **Determine the Friction Factor**:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate Pressure Loss using the Darcy-Weisbach equation**:
   Now, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   - \(L = 10 \, m\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)

   We have:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}

   Simplifying this:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
