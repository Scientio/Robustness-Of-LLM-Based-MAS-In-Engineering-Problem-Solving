LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 m\)
- \(L = 10 m\)
- \(v = 0.01 m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor (\(f\)). The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 Pa.s\) at room temperature).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas). 

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, and then determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. What do you think?

Let's proceed with calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{(1000 \, kg/m^3)(0.01 \, m/s)(0.1 \, m)}{0.001 \, Pa.s} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we will use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. 

Let's calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives us the pressure loss.

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05

   \[
