LEADER: To our team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To proceed, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and the appropriate friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) (dynamic viscosity of water) is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

After calculating Re, we can decide whether the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000), which will influence our calculation of \(f\).

Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number and discussing how to find the friction factor based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. What do you think?

ADVISOR_1: To start, let's calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the given formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

We have the following values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (velocity of water),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting these values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we can conclude that the flow is laminar.

In laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the following equation:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, substituting the value of \(Re\) into the equation for \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have calculated the friction factor, we can use it to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting in the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values back into the equation for \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
