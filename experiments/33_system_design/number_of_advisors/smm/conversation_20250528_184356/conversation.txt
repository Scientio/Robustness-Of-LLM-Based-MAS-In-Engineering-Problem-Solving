LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation for \(f\):
- For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow, we may need the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and determine the friction factor \(f\). 

Can the team assist with the calculations for Reynolds number and suggest the appropriate value for \(f\)?

ADVISOR_1: Sure! Let's begin by calculating the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the provided formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water)

Now, we can plug in the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. Calculate the numerator:
   \[
   1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}
   \]

2. Now divide by the dynamic viscosity:
   \[
   Re = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). Since \(Re < 2000\), we are in the laminar flow regime, and we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we have the friction factor \(f = 0.064\). 

Next, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D}\):
   \[
   \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
   \]

2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):
   \[
   \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

3. Now substitute these into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
