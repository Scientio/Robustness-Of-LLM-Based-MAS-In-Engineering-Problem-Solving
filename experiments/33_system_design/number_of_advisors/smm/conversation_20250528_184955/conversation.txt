LEADER: To address the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) (Reynolds number) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (at room temperature), we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\).

Let's calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m³} \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we are in the laminar flow regime.

Now, calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m³} \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Now, I’d like to hear your thoughts or any suggestions on this calculation or if there’s anything we might have overlooked regarding the friction factor or other parameters.
