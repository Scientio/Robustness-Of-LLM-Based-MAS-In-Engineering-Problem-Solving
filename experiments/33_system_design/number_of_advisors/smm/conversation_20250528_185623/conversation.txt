LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\).

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. If \(Re < 2000\), use the formula for laminar flow: \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
3. If \(Re \geq 2000\), we will need to use empirical correlations for turbulent flow.

Let's move forward with these calculations to find \(Re\) and then \(f\). What do you think about this approach?

ADVISOR_1: Your approach is sound and follows the correct steps to determine the pressure loss in the pipe using the Darcy-Weisbach equation. Let's go through the calculations step by step.

1. **Calculate the Reynolds Number \(Re\)**:

   The formula for Reynolds number is:

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   Given:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

   Plugging in the values:

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the flow type**:

   Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, the flow is laminar.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:

   For laminar flow, the friction factor is given by:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the value of \(Re\):

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)**:

   Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Substituting the known values:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
   \]

   Simplifying:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
