LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\). 

Next, we will determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), we would use empirical correlations or the Moody chart.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then decide on the appropriate friction factor. Can the team confirm the calculation of Reynolds number and the subsequent friction factor?

ADVISOR_1: To calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\), we can use the formula provided:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given the parameters:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting these values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}
\]

Now, substituting into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), we are in the laminar flow regime (Re < 2000). Therefore, we can use the laminar flow friction factor formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have the friction factor \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

1. \(\frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
