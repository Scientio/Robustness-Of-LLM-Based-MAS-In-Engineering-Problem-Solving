LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow. We can calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can compute \(Re\).

Once we know \(Re\), we can determine \(f\):
- For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations (e.g., Moody chart).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then \(f\) to proceed with the pressure loss calculation.

Team, please confirm the assumptions and calculations for \(Re\) and suggest how we should proceed with determining \(f\) and the pressure loss.

ADVISOR_1: To calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)), we can use the formula you provided:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the given values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water)

Now, substituting these values into the equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}
\]

Now, calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we are indeed in the laminar flow regime. Therefore, we can use the laminar flow friction factor formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have \(f\), we can proceed to calculate the pressure loss (\(\Delta P\)) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values we have:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss due to friction in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
