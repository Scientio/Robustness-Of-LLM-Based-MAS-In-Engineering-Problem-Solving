LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (approximately 1000 kg/m³ for water),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\),
- \(L = 10 \, m\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is about \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\).

Next, I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number and then use the appropriate formula to find \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we may need the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Team, please assist in calculating the Reynolds number and determining the friction factor based on the flow regime. What do you think?

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}

   \[

   \]

4. **Calculate the pressure loss (\(\Delta P\))**:

   Now we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Substituting in the known values:

   - \(f = 0.064\),

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{750} \approx 0.08533
