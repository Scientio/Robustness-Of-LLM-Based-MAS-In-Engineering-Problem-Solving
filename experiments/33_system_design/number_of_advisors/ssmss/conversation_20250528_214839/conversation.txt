LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (for laminar flow \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), and for turbulent flow, we might use the Moody chart or empirical equations).

Let’s calculate \(Re\) first and then discuss how to find \(f\). What do you think?

ADVISOR_1: That sounds like a solid plan! Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) using the formula you provided:

\[

Substituting in the values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). Since \(Re\) is equal to 1000, which is the transitional flow regime between laminar and turbulent flow, we will use the laminar flow equation for the friction factor, as \(Re < 2000\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have the friction factor \(f\), we can substitute all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substitute these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
