LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water in this case),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m^3\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can use the Moody chart or an appropriate empirical formula to find the friction factor \(f\) based on the Reynolds number and the relative roughness of the pipe.

Team, I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first to establish the flow regime, then find the friction factor. What are your thoughts on this approach?

ADVISOR_1: I agree with your approach. Calculating the Reynolds number first will help us determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which is crucial for finding the appropriate friction factor \(f\).

Let's start with the calculation of the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Plugging in the values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, kg/m^3 \cdot 0.01 \, m/s \cdot 0.1 \, m}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s}
\]

\[
Re = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can identify the flow regime. Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar.

For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have the friction factor, we can proceed to calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, Pa\).
