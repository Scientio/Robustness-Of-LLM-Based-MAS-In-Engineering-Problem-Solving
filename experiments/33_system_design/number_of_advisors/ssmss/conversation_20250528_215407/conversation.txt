LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature).

Next, we can use the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor, which can be determined from empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then find the friction factor.

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds Number

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\) (density of water)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\) (flow velocity)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\) (diameter of the pipe)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\) (dynamic viscosity of water)

Using the Reynolds number formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, kg/m³ \cdot 0.01 \, m/s \cdot 0.1 \, m}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s}
\]

Calculating:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, kg/(m \cdot s)}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Friction Factor

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely to be transitional between laminar and turbulent. 

1. **Laminar Flow**: For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar, and the friction factor can be calculated using:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]
   Thus, for \(Re = 1000\):
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

2. **Turbulent Flow**: If the Reynolds number were greater than 4000, we would use empirical correlations or the Moody chart to find \(f\). However, since \(Re = 1000\) is in the laminar range, we will use the laminar formula.

### Step 3: Calculate Pressure Loss

Now we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:
- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

### Conclusion

The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**. This method is robust, and using the laminar flow equation for the friction factor is appropriate given the Reynolds number.
