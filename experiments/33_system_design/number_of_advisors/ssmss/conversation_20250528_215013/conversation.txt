LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlations (for laminar or turbulent flow). 

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then find the friction factor. What do you think, team? Do you have any insights on the flow regime we might expect?

The critical Reynolds number for flow in a pipe is typically around 2000. If \(Re < 2000\), we have laminar flow; if \(Re > 4000\), we have turbulent flow. Between those values, the flow is considered transitional.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first using the given values:

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Substituting these values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we have laminar flow. 

In laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

With the friction factor determined, we can now proceed to calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation.
