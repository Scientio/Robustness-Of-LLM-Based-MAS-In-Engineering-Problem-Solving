LEADER: To the team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the flow regime, which will help us calculate the friction factor (f). The Reynolds number (Re) is given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where:
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = velocity (0.01 m/s)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (0.1 m)
- \( \mu \) = dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s)

Once we calculate Re, we can determine the friction factor (f) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \). For turbulent flow, we may use the Colebrook-White equation or other empirical correlations.

After calculating f, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss (\( \Delta P \)):

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Now, I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then proceed to find f. What do you think about this approach? Do you have any suggestions or concerns?

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
