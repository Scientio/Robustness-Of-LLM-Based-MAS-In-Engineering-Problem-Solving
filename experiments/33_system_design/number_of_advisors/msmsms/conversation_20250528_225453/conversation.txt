LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1m\),
- \(L = 10m\),
- \(v = 0.01m/s\).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \(Re\) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 Pa.s\):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Now, let's calculate \(f\) and then use it to find the pressure loss.

Team, based on this approach, do you think we are on the right track? What are your thoughts on the assumptions made, particularly regarding the friction factor?
