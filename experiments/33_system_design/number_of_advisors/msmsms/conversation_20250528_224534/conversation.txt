LEADER: To tackle the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Given the low velocity (0.01 m/s), we may expect the flow to be laminar, which simplifies the friction factor to:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, then the friction factor, and finally plug these values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

Team, please confirm if this approach is correct and if there are any considerations we should keep in mind regarding the friction factor.
