LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\):

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start with calculating the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. What do you all think?

That said, let's proceed with calculating the Reynolds number first to establish the flow regime:

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)**:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, kg/m^3 \cdot 0.01 \, m/s \cdot 0.1 \, m}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s} = \frac{1 \, kg/(m \cdot s)}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s} = 1000
\]

2. **Determine the flow regime**:
Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we have laminar flow.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:
For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. **Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation** to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, m}{0.1 \, m} \cdot \frac{1000 \, kg/m^3 \cdot (0.01 \, m/s)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step-by-step:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, Pa\).
