LEADER: Alright team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can determine the friction factor using the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart or empirical equations).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and friction factor first. I need your input on the best way to determine \(f\). Should we assume laminar flow or turbulent flow based on our Re value?
