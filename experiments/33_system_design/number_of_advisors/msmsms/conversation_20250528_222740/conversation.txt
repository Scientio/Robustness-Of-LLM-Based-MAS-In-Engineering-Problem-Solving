LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely in the transitional regime. We can estimate \(f\) using the empirical correlation for laminar flow (since Re is low):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Team, based on this calculation, do you agree with the use of the laminar flow friction factor and the assumptions made? Any suggestions for refining our approach?
