LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1m\)
- \(L = 10m\)
- \(v = 0.01m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate Re.

Next, we will use the appropriate method to find \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent).

1. If Re < 2000, the flow is laminar and \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

2. If Re > 4000, we will use the Moody chart or empirical formulas to find \(f\).

Let's calculate Re and determine \(f\). Does anyone have insights on the calculations or suggestions on estimating \(f\) based on the flow regime?

ADVISOR_2: While using \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) can simplify the calculations, it is important to note that this approximation is generally valid only for laminar flow (Re < 2000). For turbulent flow (Re > 4000), the friction factor can vary significantly and is not accurately represented by that formula.

In this case, since the flow velocity is relatively low (\(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)), it is likely that we will find ourselves in the laminar flow regime. However, it is still a good practice to calculate the Reynolds number explicitly to confirm the flow regime before applying any friction factor formula.

Let’s proceed with calculating the Reynolds number first:

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m^3\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa.s\)

Using the Reynolds number formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, the flow is indeed laminar. Therefore, we can use the laminar friction factor formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

So, the pressure loss due to friction in the pipe is \(0.32 \, Pa\). This approach confirms the laminar flow regime and provides an accurate calculation of pressure loss.
