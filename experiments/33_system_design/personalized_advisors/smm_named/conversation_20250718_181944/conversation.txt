LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we will determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (either the Moody chart or empirical formulas) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and determine the flow regime. After that, we can find \(f\) and calculate the pressure loss.

Team, how should we proceed with the calculation of \(Re\) and subsequently find \(f\)?

Lorenz: To proceed with the calculation of the Reynolds number \(Re\) and subsequently find the friction factor \(f\), we can follow these steps:

1. **Calculate the Reynolds Number \(Re\)**:
   The formula for Reynolds number is given by:

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   Plugging in the values:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

   We can calculate \(Re\):

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the Flow Regime**:
   The flow regime is determined by the value of \(Re\):
   - If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar.
   - If \(Re > 4000\), the flow is turbulent.
   - If \(2000 < Re < 4000\), the flow is in the transition region.

   Since \(Re = 1000\), the flow is laminar.

3. **Calculate the Friction Factor \(f\)**:
   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the value of \(Re\):

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Calculate the Pressure Loss \(\Delta P\)**:
   Now that we have \(f\), we can calculate the pressure loss using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Plugging in the values:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Simplifying this:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{0.1}{2}
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) due to friction in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\). 

If you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
