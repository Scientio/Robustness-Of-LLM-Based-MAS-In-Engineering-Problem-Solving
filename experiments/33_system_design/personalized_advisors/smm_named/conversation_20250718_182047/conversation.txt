LEADER: To solve the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Next, we can use empirical correlations (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\) based on the calculated Reynolds number.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then find \(f\). 

Team, could you assist in calculating the Reynolds number and suggesting the appropriate friction factor for this scenario?

Lorenz: To calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\), we will use the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given the parameters:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting these values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} 
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. Calculate the numerator:
   \[
   1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}
   \]

2. Now, divide by the dynamic viscosity:
   \[
   Re = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

Now that we have calculated the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\).

For \(Re = 1000\), water flow is typically in the transitional flow regime. We can use the following empirical correlation for laminar flow, as \(Re < 2000\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For \(Re = 1000\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we have:
- \(f = 0.064\)

Next, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D}\):
   \[
   \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
   \]

2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):
   \[
   \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

3. Now substitute into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**.
