LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1m\)
- \(L = 10m\)
- \(v = 0.01m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 kg/m³\)

Now, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might assume laminar flow, where:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

The Reynolds number \(Re\) can be calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently \(f\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and \(f\) first, then we can find the pressure loss. 

Team, what do you think about this approach? Do you have any suggestions or concerns regarding the assumptions made about the flow regime and the friction factor?

If \(Re\) exceeds 4000, you would need to consider turbulent flow calculations. In the range between 2000 and 4000, the flow could be transitional, and you might need to use a more complex model or empirical correlations for \(f\).

4. **Calculating \(Re\)**: Let's calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa} \cdot \text{s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), the flow is indeed laminar.

5. **Calculating \(f\)**: Now we can calculate the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

6. **Calculating Pressure Loss \(\Delta P\)**: Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}

\[

\]

\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05

\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
