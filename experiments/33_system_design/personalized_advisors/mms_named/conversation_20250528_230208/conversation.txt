LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's discuss the expected flow regime and how to calculate \(f\). What do you think?

Roland: Great! Let's go ahead and calculate the Reynolds number using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting the values into the equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}}
\]

Calculating this:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m.s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can use Lisa's relationship to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\),
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\).

Now substituting these values into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
