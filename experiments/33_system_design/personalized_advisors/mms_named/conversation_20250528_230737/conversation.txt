LEADER: To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. I would like your insights on which correlation to use for \(f\) and any additional considerations we should take into account.
