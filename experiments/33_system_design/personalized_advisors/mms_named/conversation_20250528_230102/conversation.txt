LEADER: To the team: We need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). Given the low velocity, it’s likely that the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

With \(\mu\) being the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa s}\)).

Let’s calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor to find the pressure loss. Any thoughts or suggestions on this approach?

Now substituting these results back:

\[
