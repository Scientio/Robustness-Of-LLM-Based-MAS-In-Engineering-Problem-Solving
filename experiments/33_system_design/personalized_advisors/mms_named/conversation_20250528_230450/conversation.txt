LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ 
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} 
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ 
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} 
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we will determine the appropriate friction factor \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[ 
f = \frac{64}{Re} 
\]

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart).

Let's calculate \(Re\) and \(f\) to find the pressure loss. Team, what do you think about this approach? Are there any concerns or additional factors we should consider?
