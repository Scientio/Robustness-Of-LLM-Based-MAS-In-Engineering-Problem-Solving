LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 m\)
- \(L = 10 m\)
- \(v = 0.01 m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Next, we can find \(f\) using empirical correlations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we would use the Colebrook-White equation or another empirical formula.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and determine the flow regime. After that, we can find \(f\) and calculate the pressure loss.

Team, could you assist in calculating the Reynolds number and determining the friction factor? Also, let’s ensure we use the appropriate correlation for \(f\) based on the flow regime we find.
