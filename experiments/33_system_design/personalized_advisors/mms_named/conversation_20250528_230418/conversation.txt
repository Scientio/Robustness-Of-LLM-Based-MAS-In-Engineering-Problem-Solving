LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1m\)
- Length \(L = 10m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Determine the friction factor \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent).
3. Finally, substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first and then find \(f\). Does anyone have insights on the expected flow regime or any additional considerations we should take into account?

- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\)

We get:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now, since we have \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the friction factor \(f\). According to my approach, we can simplify this by stating that the friction factor \(f\) is always \(25/Re\). 

So, 

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this will give us the pressure loss. Let's proceed with that!
