LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000 \]

For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Now, let's compute \(f\):

\[ f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064 \]

Now, substituting values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]

Calculating this gives:

\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 \]
\[ \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, Pa \]

I suggest we validate this calculation and consider any assumptions made, particularly regarding the friction factor and flow regime. What do you think? Is there anything we might have overlooked?
