LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\)). 

Next, we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we would use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Let's calculate Re first, and then we can decide on the friction factor \(f\). 

Team, can you confirm the viscosity of water at our operating temperature and help me calculate the Reynolds number? After that, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent and find \(f\).
