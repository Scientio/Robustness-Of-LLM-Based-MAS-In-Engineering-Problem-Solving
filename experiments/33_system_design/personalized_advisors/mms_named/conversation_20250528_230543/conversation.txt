LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 m\)
- \(L = 10 m\)
- \(v = 0.01 m/s\)

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) accordingly.

I suggest we first calculate \(Re\) and decide if the flow is laminar (Re < 2000) or turbulent (Re > 4000). If it's laminar, we can use \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). If turbulent, we may need to use the Colebrook equation or an approximation.

What do you think about this approach? Shall we proceed with these calculations?

Roland: Great! Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water)

Substituting the values into the equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001}
\]

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can use Lisa's suggested relationship for the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these results back into the equation for \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 5 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe due to friction is **0.125 Pa**.
