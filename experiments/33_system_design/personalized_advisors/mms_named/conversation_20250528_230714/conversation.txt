LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s} \)).

Let's calculate \( Re \) and then use it to find \( f \). 

I suggest we look into the Moody chart or appropriate correlations (like the Colebrook equation) for \( f \) based on our calculated Reynolds number. 

What do you think about this approach? Any additional insights on estimating the friction factor?

Roland: Excellent! Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number \( Re \) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given:
- \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) (density of water),
- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \) (velocity of water),
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \) (diameter of the pipe),
- \( \mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s} \) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, substituting these values into the equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \( Re = 1000 \), we can calculate the friction factor \( f \):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we have \( f \). Next, let's substitute \( f \), \( L \), \( D \), \( \rho \), and \( v \) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \( f = 0.025 \)
- \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \)
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
- \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)

Calculating \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term step-by-step:

1. \( \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100 \)
2. \( v^2 = (0.01)^2 = 0.0001 \)
3. \( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \)

Now substituting back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss \( \Delta P \) in the pipe is \( 0.125 \, \text{Pa} \). 

Does anyone have any questions or suggestions regarding these calculations?
