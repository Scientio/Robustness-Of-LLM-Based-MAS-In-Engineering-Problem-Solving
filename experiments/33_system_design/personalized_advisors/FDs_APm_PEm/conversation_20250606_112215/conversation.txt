LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Reynolds number and the Moody chart or appropriate empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Given the low velocity (0.01 m/s), we can calculate the Reynolds number (\(Re\)):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) first, and then we can find \(f\) and subsequently \(\Delta P\). 

I suggest we proceed by calculating \(Re\) and determining whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.
