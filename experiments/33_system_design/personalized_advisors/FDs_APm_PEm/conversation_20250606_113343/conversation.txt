LEADER: To solve the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the flow regime to find the friction factor \(f\). We can calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting everything back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating it step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
4. \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa\)

I would like to confirm with the team if the calculations and assumptions about the friction factor are correct. What do you think?
