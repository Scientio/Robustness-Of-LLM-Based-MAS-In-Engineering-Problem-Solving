\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:

- \(L = 10 \, m\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor (f). The Reynolds number is calculated as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\), we can calculate Re.

Next, we can find the friction factor (f) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow, depending on the value of Re.

- \(D = 0.1 \, m\) (diameter of the pipe),

\[

Calculating:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

2. **Determine the flow regime**:
- A Reynolds number (Re) of 1000 indicates that the flow is at the transition point between laminar and turbulent flow. For practical purposes, we can consider it as laminar flow since it's typically defined as \(Re < 2000\).

3. **Calculate the friction factor (f)**:
For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of Re:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, m\),

\[
