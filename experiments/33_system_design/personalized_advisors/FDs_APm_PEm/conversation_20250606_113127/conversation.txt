LEADER: Alright team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation for \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, but let’s calculate the Reynolds number to confirm.

Can anyone calculate the Reynolds number and suggest the appropriate friction factor for our flow conditions?

Fluid Dynamics Expert: Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number using the provided formula:

\[

Given the parameters:

- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water)

Substituting these values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}
\]

Now, divide by the dynamic viscosity:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{kg/(m·s)}} = 1000
\]

The Reynolds number is \(Re = 1000\). 

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

First, calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):

\[
\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05 \, \text{Pa}

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot 0.05
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Therefore, the pressure loss in the pipe is:

\[
\Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
