To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \( f \). Given the low velocity, we can assume laminar flow, which occurs when the Reynolds number \( Re < 2000 \). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \)). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and determine if we are in laminar flow. If we are, we can use the formula \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \).

After that, we can substitute \( f \) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

Fluid Dynamics Expert: Your approach is solid and systematic for calculating the pressure loss in the pipe using the Darcy-Weisbach equation. Let's go through the calculations step by step to ensure we have everything covered.

1. **Calculate the Reynolds Number \( Re \)**:

   The Reynolds number is given by the formula:

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   Given:
   - \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \) (density of water)
   - \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \) (velocity of water)
   - \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \) (diameter of the pipe)
   - \( \mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \) (dynamic viscosity of water)

   Substituting these values:

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

   Since \( Re < 2000 \), this indicates laminar flow.

2. **Calculate the Friction Factor \( f \)**:

   For laminar flow, the friction factor is calculated as:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the Reynolds number we calculated:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the Pressure Loss \( \Delta P \)**:

   Now we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Given:
   - \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \)
   - \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
   - \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)
   - \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)

   Substituting the values:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Simplifying:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{0.1}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \( \Delta P = 0.32 \, \text{Pa} \).
