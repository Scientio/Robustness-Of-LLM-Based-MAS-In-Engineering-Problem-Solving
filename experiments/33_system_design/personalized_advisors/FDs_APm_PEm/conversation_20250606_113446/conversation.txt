LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we first need to determine the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can compute \(Re\) and determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If \(Re \geq 2000\), we would need to use the Moody chart or other empirical correlations to find \(f\).

Now, let's calculate \(Re\) and subsequently determine \(f\).

\[

- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we can conclude that the flow is laminar. Thus, we can use the formula for the friction factor \(f\):

\[

\]

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}

3. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \, \text{Pa}\)
