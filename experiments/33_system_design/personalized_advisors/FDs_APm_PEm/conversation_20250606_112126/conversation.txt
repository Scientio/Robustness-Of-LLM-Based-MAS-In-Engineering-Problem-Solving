LEADER: To address the pressure loss in a pipe with the given dimensions and flow conditions, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Next, based on the Reynolds number, we can find the friction factor using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Fluid Dynamics Expert: Your approach is sound! Let's go through the steps systematically to calculate the Reynolds number, find the friction factor, and then use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to determine the pressure loss.

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds Number

The formula for the Reynolds number \(Re\) is:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Substituting the values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Friction Factor

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is typically in the transitional regime between laminar and turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

### Step 3: Calculate the Pressure Loss

Now that we have the friction factor, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Simplifying the equation step-by-step:

1. Calculate \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\).
2. Calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\).

Now, substituting these values back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Conclusion

The pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**. If you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
