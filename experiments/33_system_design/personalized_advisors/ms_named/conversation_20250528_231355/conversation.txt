LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1m\),
- Length \(L = 10m\),
- Velocity \(v = 0.01m/s\),
- Density \(\rho \approx 1000 kg/m³\).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is transitioning between laminar and turbulent. In this case, we can use the approximation for laminar flow since \(Re < 2000\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute everything into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

I would like to confirm if the team agrees with this approach and calculations, particularly regarding the friction factor \(f\) and the assumptions made about the flow regime. What are your thoughts?
