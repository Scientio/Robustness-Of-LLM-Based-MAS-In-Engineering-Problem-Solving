LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we'll use the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\) based on \(Re\).

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, then determine \(f\), and finally use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\). 

What do you think about this approach? Do you have any suggestions or additional considerations?

Roland: Roland: Lisa, I agree that using the relationship \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) is a valid simplification for laminar flow conditions (where \(Re < 2000\)). Given that we are working with water and relatively low velocities, this approach can indeed save us time.

Let's proceed with your suggestion. We'll first calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) is less than 2000, we are in the laminar flow regime. Now we can calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now that we have \(f\), we can substitute it into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
