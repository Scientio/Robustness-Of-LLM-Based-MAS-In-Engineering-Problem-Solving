LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m,
- \(L = 10\) m,
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) for water of about \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Next, we will use the appropriate correlation to find \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Now, team, let’s calculate the Reynolds number and determine the friction factor. What are your thoughts on the flow regime and the method to find \(f\)?

3. **Now, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[

   \]

   Thus:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]
