LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water in this case, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\):

Now, let’s calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor \(f\). If the flow is laminar (Re < 2000), we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If the flow is turbulent, we would need to use the Moody chart or an empirical correlation.

Team, let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the friction factor. After that, we can compute the pressure loss. What do you think?

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting in the values we have:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\)

Now, let's calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, kg/m³ \times 0.01 \, m/s \times 0.1 \, m}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s}
\]

Calculating this step by step:

1. Calculate the numerator: \(1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1 = 1 \, kg/(m \cdot s)\)
2. Divide by the dynamic viscosity: 

\[
Re = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number, we can calculate the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

With \(f\) calculated, we can now use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting in the values:

- \(f = 0.025\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, kg/m³\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

Now, let's calculate \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now, substituting these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, Pa\).

Given our Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we are in the laminar flow regime (Re < 2000). Therefore, the correct friction factor should be:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, let's recalculate the pressure loss using the correct friction factor:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the correct value of \(f\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

Thus, the correct pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, Pa\).
