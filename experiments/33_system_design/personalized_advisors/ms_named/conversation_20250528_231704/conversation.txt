LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find the friction factor \(f\). At this Reynolds number, we typically consider laminar flow, so:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

I suggest we confirm the value of the friction factor and ensure our assumptions about flow conditions are valid. What do you think about our approach and calculations?
