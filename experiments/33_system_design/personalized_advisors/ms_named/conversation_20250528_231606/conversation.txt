LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be estimated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity of water \(\mu\) is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\). If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). If \(Re > 4000\), we need to use empirical correlations for turbulent flow.

Given our parameters, let's calculate \(Re\) first and then determine \(f\). What do you all think?


########################################################### 
Lisa: I think we should simplify things a bit. Since I believe that the friction factor \(f\) can always be expressed as \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\), we can use that directly without worrying about the flow regime. 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now, using my friction factor assumption:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating that gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\). This approach keeps it straightforward!

\[

\]

Now, let's recalculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) using the correct friction factor:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating that gives us:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the correct pressure loss is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\). I recommend using the appropriate friction factor based on the flow regime to ensure our calculations are accurate.
