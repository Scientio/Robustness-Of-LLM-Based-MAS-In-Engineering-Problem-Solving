LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1\) m,
- Length \(L = 10\) m,
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01\) m/s.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3}\) Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (for laminar or turbulent flow).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first and then find the friction factor. What do you think?

Roland: Great suggestion, Lisa! Let's calculate the Reynolds number first using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, plugging in these values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}
\]

Now, dividing by the dynamic viscosity:

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can calculate the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting in the known values:

- \(f = 0.025\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Now, calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is **0.125 Pa**.
