LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)).

After calculating the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart) to find \(f\).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, and then determine the appropriate friction factor. What do you think?

First, we will calculate the Reynolds number using the formula:

\[

\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now that we have \(Re = 1000\), we can calculate the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.025\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term:

\[
\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
\]

\[
\frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \times 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05
\]

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
