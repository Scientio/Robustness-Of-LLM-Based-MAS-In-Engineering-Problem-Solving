LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. The Reynolds number \(Re\) can help us with that:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water around 0.001 Pa·s, we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently determine \(f\). 

Let's calculate \(Re\) and decide on the friction factor. Any thoughts on how we should proceed with this?

Lisa: Absolutely, we can proceed by calculating the Reynolds number \(Re\).

- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[

\]

Now, since \(Re = 1000\), we can use my unique approach for the friction factor \(f\):

\[

\]

Now that we have \(f\), we can plug it into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\). Shall we continue with that calculation?

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
