LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³), for water approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \(Re\) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\), we can plug in the values.

Next, we can calculate \(Re\) and subsequently find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Team, I need your input on the following:
1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Determine the friction factor \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent).
3. Finally, use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to compute the pressure loss.

What do you think?

\[

\[

\]
