LEADER: To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction in a pipe can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left( \frac{L}{D} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \right)
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature). 

Since the flow velocity is low, we may expect laminar flow, which means we can use the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let’s calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor. After that, we can plug in the values to find the pressure loss. 

Team, what do you think about this approach? Should we proceed with these calculations?

   \[

2. **Calculate \(Re\)**:
   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
   \]
   Since \(Re = 1000\), we are indeed in the laminar flow regime.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:
   Using the laminar flow formula:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Now, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \left( \frac{L}{D} \right) \cdot \left( \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \right)
   \]
   where:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

5. **Calculate \(\Delta P\)**:
   - First, calculate \(\frac{L}{D}\):
   \[
   \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
   \]
   - Then, calculate \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):
   \[
   \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \times 0.0001}{2} = 0.05
   \]

6. **Putting it all together**:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
