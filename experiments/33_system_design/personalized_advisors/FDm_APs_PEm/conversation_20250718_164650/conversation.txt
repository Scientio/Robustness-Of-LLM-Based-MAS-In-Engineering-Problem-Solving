The pressure loss in a pipe can be determined using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)).

Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, which means we can use the formula for \( f \) in laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and subsequently the friction factor. After that, we can compute the pressure loss.
