To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\right)
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Given the low velocity, we might expect laminar flow, which means \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then the friction factor. After that, we can substitute back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. 

What do you all think about this approach? Any suggestions or concerns?

Let's proceed step by step:

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number**:
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]
   Substituting the known values:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the friction factor \(f\)**:
   Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we are indeed in the laminar flow regime. Therefore, we can use:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)**:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\right)
   \]
   Substituting the values:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \left(\frac{10}{0.1}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}\right)
   \]

   Simplifying further:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \left(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}\right) = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Therefore, the pressure loss in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**.
