LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we may be dealing with laminar flow (Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\)). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Once we establish whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, we can find \(f\) accordingly:
- For laminar flow, \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).
- For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or charts.

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and then find \(f\). What are your thoughts on this approach?

   \]
