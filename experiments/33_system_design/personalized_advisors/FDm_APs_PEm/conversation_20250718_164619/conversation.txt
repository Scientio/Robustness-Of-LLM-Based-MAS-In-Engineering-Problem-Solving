LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of the fluid (kg/m³) - for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \( D = 0.1 \) m
- \( L = 10 \) m
- \( v = 0.01 \) m/s

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \( f \):

\[ \text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) for water at room temperature is approximately \( 0.001 \) Pa·s, we can calculate Re.

Next, we will use the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical equations) to find the friction factor based on the Reynolds number and the relative roughness of the pipe.

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor. What do you think? Should we proceed with this approach?

Now, let's calculate the Reynolds number using the formula:

\[
\text{Re} = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
- \( \mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)

Calculating:

\[
\text{Re} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now, using our friction factor relationship:

\[
f = \frac{25}{\text{Re}} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

We can now plug this value back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. Shall we go ahead with that?

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05

   \[

   \]
