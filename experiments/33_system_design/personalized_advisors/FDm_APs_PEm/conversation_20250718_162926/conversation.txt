LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the appropriate friction factor. The Reynolds number is given by:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature). 

Once we calculate the Reynolds number, we can determine the friction factor \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Let’s calculate the Reynolds number and then find the friction factor. What do you all think? Any suggestions on how to approach the friction factor calculation?

Applied Physicist: That sounds like a practical approach! Using the approximation \( f = \frac{25}{Re} \) will indeed simplify our calculations. Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number using the formula:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Given:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, substituting the values into the Reynolds number equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} 
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can calculate the friction factor \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10 \, \text{m}}{0.1 \, \text{m}} \cdot \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot (0.01 \, \text{m/s})^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each term step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(v^2 = (0.01)^2 = 0.0001 \, \text{m}^2/\text{s}^2\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is **0.125 Pa**. 

Does everyone agree with these calculations, or is there anything else we should consider?
