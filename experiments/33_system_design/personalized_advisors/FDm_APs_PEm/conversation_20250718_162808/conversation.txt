LEADER: To the team, we have a problem regarding the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To determine the friction factor \(f\), we need to know if the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. 

1. Calculate \(Re\):
   - \(Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000\)

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use \(f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064\).

2. Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Let's compute this value. 

I would like your advice on whether there are any considerations or alternative methods we should take into account before finalizing our calculations.

\[

\[
