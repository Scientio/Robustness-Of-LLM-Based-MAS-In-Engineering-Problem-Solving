To solve this, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of water.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can use it to find the friction factor, which can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the friction factor. I would like your input on the best way to approach finding the friction factor, considering the flow regime.
