LEADER: Let's break down the problem of calculating the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction. We have a pipe with a diameter (D) of 0.1 m, a length (L) of 10 m, and a water flow velocity (v) of 0.01 m/s.

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using the Moody chart or empirical correlations. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), we use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and we can use the friction factor formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \times 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

I would like to confirm this approach with the team. Do you agree with the calculations and the method used to determine the pressure loss?
