LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\).

Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we would need to use empirical correlations or charts (like the Moody chart).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and decide on the flow regime. What do you all think about this approach? Should we proceed with the calculations?

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}}
   \]

   \]

2. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:

   Using the assumption \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\):

   \[
   f = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
   \]

3. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

   Given:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

   The pressure loss is calculated as:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Plugging in the values:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   \[
   = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]

   \[
   = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]

   \[
   = 0.025 \cdot 5 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately **0.125 Pa**.
