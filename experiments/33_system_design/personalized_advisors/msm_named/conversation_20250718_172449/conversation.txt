LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we can determine the friction factor \(f\) based on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we can use empirical correlations like the Colebrook equation.

Let's calculate \(Re\) first and then decide on \(f\). What do you think about this approach?

3. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)**:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

\[

Calculating this step-by-step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}

Thus, the estimated pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
