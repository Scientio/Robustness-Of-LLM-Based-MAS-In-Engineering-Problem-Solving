LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\right)
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is low, we might be dealing with laminar flow. In laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, defined as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Here, \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Let's calculate \(Re\) first and then find \(f\). After that, we can compute the pressure loss. 

I suggest we proceed with these calculations. What do you think?

Given:

Substituting the values:

\[

\[

\]

3. **Now, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \left(\frac{L}{D}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\right)
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\frac{L}{D}\):

\[
\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
\]

Calculating \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2}\):

\[
\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05
\]

Now, substituting everything back into the equation for \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]
