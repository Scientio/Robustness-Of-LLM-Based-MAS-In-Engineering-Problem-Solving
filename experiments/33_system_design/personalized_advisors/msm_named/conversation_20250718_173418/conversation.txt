LEADER: To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (either the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

I suggest we calculate \(Re\) first, then determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. 

What are your thoughts on this approach?

   \[

   \]

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Calculate \(f\)**:
   \[
   f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
   \]

3. **Now, we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}

   \]

   Simplifying:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\).
