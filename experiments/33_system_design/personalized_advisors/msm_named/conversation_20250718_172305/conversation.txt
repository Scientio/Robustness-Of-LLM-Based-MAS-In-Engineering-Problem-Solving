LEADER: Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to find the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature, we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart for turbulent or laminar flow).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number and then determine \(f\). What are your thoughts on this approach?

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
