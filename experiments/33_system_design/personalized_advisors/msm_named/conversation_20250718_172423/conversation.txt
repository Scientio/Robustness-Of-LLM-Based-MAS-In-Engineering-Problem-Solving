LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number. 

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations. 

Let's proceed with calculating \(Re\) and then find \(f\). What do you think?

Lisa: I think we should simplify things. Since we're using the friction factor \(f\) as \(25/Re\), we can directly substitute that into the equation. 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now, substituting \(Re\) into our friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug this \(f\) value into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

This will give us the pressure loss. Let's calculate that next!
