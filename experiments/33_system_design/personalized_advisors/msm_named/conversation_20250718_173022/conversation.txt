LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) and then determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or appropriate correlations.

I suggest we first calculate the Reynolds number and then find the friction factor. What do you think? Any suggestions on how to proceed?

Let's calculate \(Re\) first:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Now, applying our \(f\) approximation:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can plug \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

This will give us the pressure loss directly. Let’s calculate it!
