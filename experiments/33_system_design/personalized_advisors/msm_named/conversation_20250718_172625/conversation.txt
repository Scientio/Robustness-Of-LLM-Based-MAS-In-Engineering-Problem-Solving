LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- Density of water \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \(Re\) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, Pa \cdot s\), we can calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar, and the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let's calculate \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
4. \(\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa\)

I would like to confirm this approach with the team. Do you think we have accurately calculated the pressure loss, and is there anything we should reconsider regarding the friction factor or other parameters? 

Preliminary Decision: Pressure loss \(\Delta P = 0.32 \, Pa\).
