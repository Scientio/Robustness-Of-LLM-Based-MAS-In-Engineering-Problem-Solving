LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where the water flow velocity is 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \( f \):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is about \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s} \), we can calculate \( Re \).

Once we have \( Re \), we can find \( f \) using the appropriate correlation (either the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first and then determine the friction factor. Does anyone have insights on the best way to find \( f \) for our flow conditions?
