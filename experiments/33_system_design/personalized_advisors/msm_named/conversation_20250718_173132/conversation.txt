LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

After we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow. Given the low flow velocity, we might expect laminar flow, where \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\).

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute all values back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

What do you think about this approach? Do you have any suggestions or modifications?

Lorenz: That’s a good suggestion, Lisa! Using \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) simplifies the process and allows us to move forward more efficiently. Let's proceed with the calculations.

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)**:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

2. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:

Using the simplified formula:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

3. **Substitute values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation**:

Now we can calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step-by-step:

- \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
- \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
- \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(\Delta P = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}\). 

Does this look good to everyone? Any further modifications or questions?
