LEADER: Alright team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we need to consider the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

Given the low velocity, we might be dealing with laminar flow, so we can use the formula for the friction factor in laminar flow:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

where \( Re \) is the Reynolds number, calculated as:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Here, \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s).

Let's calculate \( Re \) first and then find \( f \). After that, we can compute the pressure loss.

What do you think about this approach? Any suggestions or insights on the calculations?

First, we'll calculate the Reynolds number \( Re \):

Given:

- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \) (flow velocity)
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \) (diameter of the pipe)
- \( \mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \) (dynamic viscosity of water)

We can calculate \( Re \) using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now, we can calculate the friction factor \( f \):

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Next, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \( L = 10 \, \text{m} \)
- \( D = 0.1 \, \text{m} \)
- \( \rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)
- \( v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \)

Calculating \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating step-by-step:

1. \( \frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100 \)
2. \( \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05 \)

Now substituting these values back into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So the pressure loss in the pipe is \( 0.125 \, \text{Pa} \).
