LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01m/s)

Since the flow velocity is quite low, we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent to find the correct friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) will help us with this:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)). 

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and determine the flow regime to find \(f\). After that, we can compute the pressure loss. 

I’d like your input on how to proceed with calculating the Reynolds number and determining the friction factor.


########################################################### 
Lisa: Sure! Let's calculate the Reynolds number first. The formula is:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu \approx 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Calculating:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since the Reynolds number is 1000, the flow is transitional. However, we can use the friction factor \(f\) as \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) for simplicity.

So:

\[
f = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating further:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
= 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is 0.125 Pa. Let me know if you need any further details!
