LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1m\)
- Length \(L = 10m\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 kg/m³\)

The friction factor \(f\) depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent) and can be determined using the Reynolds number \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 Pa \cdot s\) at room temperature). 

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Determine \(f\) based on \(Re\) (use \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\) for laminar flow, \(Re < 2000\)).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

Now, I would like your input on the expected flow regime and any additional considerations we should take into account for calculating \(f\). What do you think?
