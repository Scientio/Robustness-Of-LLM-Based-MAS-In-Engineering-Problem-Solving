LEADER: To the team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

The pressure loss due to friction can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to find the appropriate friction factor (\(f\)):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlations for \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Given the low velocity, I suspect the flow might be laminar. Let's calculate \(Re\) and confirm the flow regime. 

Can anyone provide insights on calculating \(Re\) and determining \(f\) based on our findings?
