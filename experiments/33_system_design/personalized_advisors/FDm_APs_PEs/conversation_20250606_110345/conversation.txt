LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

1. We need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow, \(f\) can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number, given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

2. Let's calculate \(Re\):
   - \(\rho \approx 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(\mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\)

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

3. Now, we can calculate \(f\):
\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\):
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
= 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
= 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Now, I would like to hear your thoughts on this calculation and if you have any suggestions or corrections regarding the approach, especially concerning the friction factor \(f\).
