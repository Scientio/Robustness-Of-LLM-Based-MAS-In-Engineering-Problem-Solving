LEADER: To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor (f). The Reynolds number (Re) is calculated using the formula:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho \cdot v \cdot D}{\mu} \]

Where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Next, we can determine the friction factor (f) using the appropriate correlations based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \( f \) can be calculated as:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

For turbulent flow, we would typically use the Moody chart or empirical correlations. 

Once we have the friction factor, we can calculate the pressure loss using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho \cdot v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m).

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor. What are your thoughts on this approach?
