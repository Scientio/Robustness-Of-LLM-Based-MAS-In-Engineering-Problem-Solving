LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{(1000 \, kg/m^3) \cdot (0.01 \, m/s) \cdot (0.1 \, m)}{0.001 \, Pa \cdot s} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate the friction factor \(f\). 

A common approximation for laminar flow (Re < 2000) is:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Thus:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, Pa
\]

I suggest we use this approach to confirm our calculations and assumptions. What do you all think about this method and the friction factor chosen? Any additional insights?
