LEADER: Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given that the water flow velocity is 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to calculate the friction factor \( f \). Given the low velocity, we might be dealing with laminar flow, which occurs when the Reynolds number \( Re < 2000 \).

The Reynolds number is calculated as follows:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Using the dynamic viscosity of water (\( \mu \approx 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \)), we can find \( Re \) and then determine \( f \) using:

- For laminar flow: \( f = \frac{64}{Re} \)
- For turbulent flow, we would need to use the Moody chart or an empirical formula.

I suggest we first calculate the Reynolds number to confirm the flow regime. What do you all think? Any additional thoughts on determining \( f \)?
