LEADER: To determine the pressure loss in the pipe, we need to use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to find the Reynolds number (Re) to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\):

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. Depending on the value of \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations for laminar or turbulent flow. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\). For turbulent flow, we would use the appropriate empirical formula.

Once we have \(f\), we can substitute it back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss.

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and then proceed to find the friction factor. What do you all think?
