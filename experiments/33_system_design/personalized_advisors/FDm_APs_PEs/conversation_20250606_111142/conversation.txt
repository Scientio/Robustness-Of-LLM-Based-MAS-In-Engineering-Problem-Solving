LEADER: To solve the problem of pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³) - for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1\) m
- \(L = 10\) m
- \(v = 0.01\) m/s

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely to be laminar, and we can use:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, substituting \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]
\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

I suggest we confirm the calculations and ensure we understand the assumptions made, particularly regarding the flow regime and the friction factor. What do you all think? Is there anything we should consider further?
