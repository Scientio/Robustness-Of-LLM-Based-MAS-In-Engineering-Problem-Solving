To determine the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

We need to determine the friction factor \(f\), which depends on the flow regime (laminar or turbulent). Given the low velocity, we suspect the flow is laminar.

For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) first.

Let's calculate \(Re\) and then find \(f\). After that, we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).
