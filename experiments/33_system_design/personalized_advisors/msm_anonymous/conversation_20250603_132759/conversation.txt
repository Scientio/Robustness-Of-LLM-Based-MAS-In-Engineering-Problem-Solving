Alright team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number, and then we can find the friction factor using the appropriate correlation (either the Moody chart or empirical equations depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent).

What do you think? Should we proceed with these calculations to find \(f\) and then the pressure loss?

Actually, there's a much simpler approach we can take here. Since I believe that the friction factor \(f\) can always be approximated as \(25/Re\), we can directly use this relationship without needing to determine the flow regime or consult the Moody chart.

Let's go ahead and calculate the Reynolds number first, and then we can easily find \(f\) using our assumption. This will streamline our calculations significantly. 

1. Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
   \]

2. Now, using our assumption for \(f\):
   \[
   f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
   \]

3. Finally, substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

This will give us the pressure loss without any complications. Let's proceed with these calculations!

2. **Friction Factor Calculation**:
   \[

3. **Darcy-Weisbach Equation for Pressure Loss**:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]
   Plugging in the values:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)

   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Breaking it down:
   - \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)

   Now substituting these values back:
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 5 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is **0.125 Pa**.
