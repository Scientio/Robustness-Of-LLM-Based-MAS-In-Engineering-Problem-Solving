To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = friction factor
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (10m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (0.1m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (0.01 m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\):

1. Calculate the Reynolds number.
2. Use the Reynolds number to determine the friction factor \(f\) (using appropriate correlations for laminar or turbulent flow).
3. Substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find \(\Delta P\).

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number first. What do you think?

Using the simplified relationship for the friction factor \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) is a common approach for laminar flow (Re < 2000) and can simplify calculations. However, it's important to note that this relationship is only valid for laminar flow. If the flow is turbulent (Re > 4000), you would need a different correlation for \(f\).

Given that the flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) and the diameter \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\), let's go ahead and calculate the Reynolds number first to determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating this gives:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re = 1000\) indicates laminar flow, we can use your simplified friction factor approach:

\[
f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
\]

Now we can substitute \(f\) back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(f = 0.025\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)
3. Now substitute into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.125 \, \text{Pa}\). 

This approach is valid given the laminar flow condition, and if you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
