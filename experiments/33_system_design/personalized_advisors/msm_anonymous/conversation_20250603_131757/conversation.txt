To our team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe due to friction. The parameters we have are:

- Diameter (D) = 0.1 m
- Length (L) = 10 m
- Flow velocity (v) = 0.01 m/s

The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³).

To find the friction factor \(f\), we need to determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent. Since the flow velocity is low, we might expect laminar flow. We can calculate the Reynolds number (Re):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now, let’s calculate \(f\) and then use it to find the pressure loss. 

I’d like to hear your thoughts on this approach and if you agree with calculating the friction factor this way.
