To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the pipe length (10m),
- \(D\) is the pipe diameter (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor \(f\).

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming a dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water at room temperature is approximately \(0.001 \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

After finding \(Re\), we can then determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations.

Let's calculate \(Re\) first.
