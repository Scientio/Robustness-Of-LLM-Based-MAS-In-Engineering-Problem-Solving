Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, given a water flow velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To find the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical formulas).

\[

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{\rho v^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = 0.005\)

Now plug these values into the equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.005 = 0.0125 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) is \(0.0125 \, \text{Pa}\). This method is straightforward and effective! What do you think?
