Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate the Reynolds number.

Next, we can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\) based on the Reynolds number and the pipe's roughness.

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, then find the friction factor, and finally use it to calculate the pressure loss. What do you think?

Actually, there's a simpler approach we can take. Instead of calculating the Reynolds number and looking up the friction factor, we can directly use the relationship that \(f = \frac{25}{Re}\) for our calculations.

Let's proceed with that:

1. Calculate the Reynolds number:
   \[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

   \[ f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025 \]

3. Finally, plug this into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[ \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} \]
