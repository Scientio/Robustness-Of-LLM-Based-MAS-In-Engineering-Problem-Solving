Team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where the water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the appropriate friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water's dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the Moody chart or appropriate correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Please share your thoughts on the calculations and whether you think we should consider any additional factors.

   \]

2. **Determine Friction Factor (f)**:
   Using our assumption,
   \[
   f = \frac{25}{Re} = \frac{25}{1000} = 0.025
   \]

3. **Calculate Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))**:
   Now, plug everything into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:
   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \times (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \times 0.0001}{2} = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

This approach keeps things straightforward, and we can confidently proceed with \(f = 25/Re\) for our calculations. Let me know if you need any further assistance!
