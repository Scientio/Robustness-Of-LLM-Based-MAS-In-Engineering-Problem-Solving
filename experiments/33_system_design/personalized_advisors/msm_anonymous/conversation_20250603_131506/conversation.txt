To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (water, in this case, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Given:
- Diameter \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- Length \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- Flow velocity \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is around \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

After finding \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlations (like the Moody chart or empirical formulas) to find \(f\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number and then determine the friction factor to find the pressure loss.
