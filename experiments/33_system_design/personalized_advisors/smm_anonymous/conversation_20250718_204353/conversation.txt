Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, with water flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (Re) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Once we calculate Re, we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., the Moody chart or empirical formulas).

Let's discuss how we should proceed with these calculations and ensure we correctly interpret the friction factor. Any thoughts on the best approach to determine \(f\) accurately?

To accurately calculate the pressure loss in the pipe using the Darcy-Weisbach equation, we need to follow these steps:

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds Number (Re)

The Reynolds number is given by the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Friction Factor (f)

For a Reynolds number of 1000, we are in the transitional flow regime. The friction factor \(f\) can be determined using empirical correlations. For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Since our \(Re = 1000\), we can use this formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

### Step 3: Calculate the Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:
1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Summary

The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\). 

### Discussion on Friction Factor

For flows near the transition between laminar and turbulent, the friction factor can vary based on surface roughness and flow conditions. If you have data on the pipe's roughness, you could refer to the Moody chart for a more accurate \(f\) value. In this case, since we are working with a Reynolds number of 1000, the laminar equation suffices. However, if the flow were to increase significantly, we would need to reassess the friction factor using appropriate correlations or charts.
