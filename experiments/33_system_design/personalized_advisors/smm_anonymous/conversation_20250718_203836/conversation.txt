Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we will use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\).

Once we have \(Re\), we can determine \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow. 

Let’s discuss this approach and confirm the calculations for \(f\) based on the flow regime. What do you think?

Your approach to calculating the pressure loss in the pipe using the Darcy-Weisbach equation is sound. Let's break down the steps and confirm the calculations for the friction factor \(f\) based on the flow regime.

### Step 1: Calculate the Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Plugging in the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (velocity),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter),
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity).

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

### Step 2: Determine the Flow Regime

The flow regime can be classified as:
- Laminar flow if \(Re < 2000\),
- Transitional flow if \(2000 \leq Re < 4000\),
- Turbulent flow if \(Re \geq 4000\).

Since \(Re = 1000\), the flow is laminar.

### Step 3: Calculate the Friction Factor \(f\)

For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) is given by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Calculating \(f\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

### Step 4: Calculate the Pressure Loss \(\Delta P\)

Now we can substitute \(f\), \(L\), \(D\), \(\rho\), and \(v\) into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting in the values:
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

### Conclusion

The pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\). Your approach and calculations are correct, and the steps outlined will lead to accurate results for this problem. If you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
