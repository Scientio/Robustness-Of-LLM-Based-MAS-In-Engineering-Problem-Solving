To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Next, we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation for laminar or turbulent flow, depending on the Reynolds number.

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime and then find \(f\). 

Team, please advise on the calculation of the Reynolds number and the appropriate friction factor correlation to use.

Let's start by calculating the Reynolds number using the formula provided:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:

- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Now, substituting these values into the equation:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \cdot 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \cdot 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

Now that we have the Reynolds number \(Re = 1000\), we can determine the flow regime. Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar.

For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the calculated Reynolds number:

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now that we have the friction factor, we can substitute all values back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:

- \(f = 0.064\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each component:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \(\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05\)

Now substituting these values back:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
