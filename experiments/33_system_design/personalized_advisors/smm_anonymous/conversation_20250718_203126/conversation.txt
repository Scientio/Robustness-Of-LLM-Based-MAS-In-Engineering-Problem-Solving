Alright team, we need to determine the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1m and a length of 10m, where water flows at a velocity of 0.01m/s. 

To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the flow regime and find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) at room temperature).

Next, we can use the appropriate correlation to find \(f\). 

Can anyone suggest how we should proceed with calculating \(Re\) and determining \(f\) for our flow conditions?

ADVISOR_1
To calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) and subsequently determine the friction factor \(f\), we can follow these steps:

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)**:

   The formula for the Reynolds number is given by:

   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]

   Given:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (velocity of water),
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

   Plugging in the values:

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

   So, \(Re = 1000\).

2. **Determine the flow regime**:

   Since \(Re < 2000\), the flow is considered laminar. For laminar flow, the friction factor \(f\) can be calculated using the formula:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the value of \(Re\):

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)**:

   Now that we have \(f\), we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Substituting the known values:

   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

   The equation becomes:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Simplifying:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
   \]
   \[
   = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**.
