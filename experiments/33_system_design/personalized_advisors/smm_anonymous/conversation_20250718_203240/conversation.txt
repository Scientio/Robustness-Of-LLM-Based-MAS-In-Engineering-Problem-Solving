The pressure loss can be calculated using the Darcy-Weisbach equation: 

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa.s}\)).

Once we have \(Re\), we can use the appropriate correlation (like the Moody chart) to find the friction factor, which depends on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

I suggest we start by calculating the Reynolds number and determining if the flow is laminar (\(Re < 2000\)) or turbulent (\(Re > 4000\)). After that, we can find \(f\) and calculate the pressure loss.

What are your thoughts on this approach, and do you have any suggestions on how to proceed, especially regarding the friction factor?
