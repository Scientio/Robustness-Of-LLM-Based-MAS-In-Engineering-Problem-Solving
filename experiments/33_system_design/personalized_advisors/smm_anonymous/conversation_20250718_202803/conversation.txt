To determine the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe (10m),
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1m),
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s).

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\), we can calculate \(Re\).

Next, we need to determine \(f\) based on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

For turbulent flow, \(f\) can be found using empirical correlations like the Moody chart.

I suggest we first calculate the Reynolds number to classify the flow and then find the appropriate friction factor. What do you think about this approach?

1. **Calculate the Reynolds Number (\(Re\))**:

   \[

   \]

   Given:
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (flow velocity),
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
   - \(\mu = 0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

   Plugging in the values:

   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = \frac{1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the Flow Regime**:

   Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, the flow is classified as laminar.

3. **Calculate the Friction Factor (\(f\))**:

   For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using the formula:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re}
   \]

   Substituting the Reynolds number:

   \[
   f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

4. **Calculate the Pressure Loss (\(\Delta P\))**:

   Now that we have \(f\), we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss:

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   Given:
   - \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
   - \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
   - \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
   - \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

   Plugging in the values:

   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]

   Therefore:

   \[

   \]

So, the pressure loss in the pipe is \(0.32 \, \text{Pa}\).
