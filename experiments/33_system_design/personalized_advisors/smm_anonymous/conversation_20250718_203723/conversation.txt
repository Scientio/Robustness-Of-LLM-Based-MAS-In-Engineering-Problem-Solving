To solve this, we'll use the Darcy-Weisbach equation for pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/mÂ³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming water at room temperature, the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Next, we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation, such as the Moody chart or the Colebrook equation, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent.

Given our low velocity, it's likely that the flow is laminar (Re < 2000), which means \(f\) can be approximated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Let's calculate \(Re\) and \(f\) to find the pressure loss. Any thoughts on this approach?
