Let's break down the problem of pressure loss in the pipe. We have a pipe with a diameter (D) of 0.1m, a length (L) of 10m, and water flowing through it at a velocity (v) of 0.01m/s. 

To calculate the pressure loss, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

Next, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Where \(Re\) is the Reynolds number given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity of water (\(\mu\)) is approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\), we can calculate \(Re\) to see if the flow is laminar or turbulent.

1. Calculate \(Re\).
2. If \(Re < 2000\), use the laminar formula for \(f\). If \(Re \geq 2000\), we can use empirical correlations for turbulent flow.

Let's calculate \(Re\) first and then decide on the friction factor. What do you think?

Let's proceed step by step to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) and then determine the friction factor \(f\).

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\)**:

The formula for Reynolds number is:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Substituting the values:
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) (density of water),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\) (velocity of water),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\) (diameter of the pipe),
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) (dynamic viscosity of water).

Now, plug in the values:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \times 0.01 \, \text{m/s} \times 0.1 \, \text{m}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}}
\]

Calculating the numerator:

\[
1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1 = 1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}
\]

Now calculate \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1 \, \text{kg/(m·s)}}{1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}} = 1000
\]

2. **Determine the flow regime**:

Since \(Re = 1000\), which is less than 2000, we confirm that the flow is laminar.

3. **Calculate the friction factor \(f\)**:

Using the laminar flow formula for the friction factor:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

4. **Calculate the pressure loss \(\Delta P\)** using the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

Now, we can substitute the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the known values:
- \(f = 0.064\),
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\),
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\),
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\),
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\).

Calculating \(\Delta P\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating each part:

\[
\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100
\]

\[
\frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2} = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05
\]

Now putting it all together:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 5 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

So, the pressure loss \(\Delta P\) in the pipe is approximately **0.32 Pa**. If you have any further questions or need additional calculations, feel free to ask!
