\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Calculating the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, we can conclude that the flow is laminar. In this case, we can use the Hagen-Poiseuille equation to calculate the pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = \frac{8 \mu L Q}{\pi R^4} \]

where:
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( Q \) is the volumetric flow rate, which can be calculated as \( Q = A \times v \) with \( A = \frac{\pi D^2}{4} \),
- \( R \) is the radius of the pipe (0.05 m).

We should proceed to calculate the volumetric flow rate and then use it in the Hagen-Poiseuille equation to find the pressure loss."
