\[ Re = \frac{\rho V D}{\mu} \]

where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( V \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s).

Calculating the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

\[ \Delta P = \frac{8 \mu L Q}{\pi R^4} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( Q \) is the volumetric flow rate, which can be calculated as \( Q = A \times V = \frac{\pi D^2}{4} \times V \),
- \( R \) is the radius of the pipe (0.05 m).

Calculating the volumetric flow rate:

\[ Q = \frac{\pi (0.1)^2}{4} \times 0.01 = \frac{\pi \times 0.01}{4} \approx 0.00785 \, m^3/s \]

Now substituting the values into the Hagen-Poiseuille equation:

\[ \Delta P = \frac{8 \times 0.001 \times 10 \times 0.00785}{\pi (0.05)^4} \]

Calculating the denominator:

\[ \pi (0.05)^4 = \pi \times 0.00000625 \approx 0.0000196349 \]

Now substituting this back into the equation for pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P \approx \frac{8 \times 0.001 \times 10 \times 0.00785}{0.0000196349} \]

Calculating this gives us the pressure loss in Pascals. 

To summarize, we first calculated the Reynolds number to confirm laminar flow, followed by using the Hagen-Poiseuille equation to find the pressure loss. The material and roughness of the pipe are less critical in laminar flow as compared to turbulent flow, but they could still be considered for a more comprehensive analysis. 

I would suggest we proceed with the calculation to find the exact pressure loss value."
