The Reynolds number (Re) can be calculated using the formula:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho \cdot v \cdot D}{\mu} \]

where:
- \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the flow velocity (0.01 m/s),
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe (0.1 m),
- \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s at room temperature).

Plugging in the values, we get:

\[ Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000 \]

Since the Reynolds number is less than 2000, this indicates that the flow is laminar.

For laminar flow, we can use the Hagen-Poiseuille equation to calculate the pressure loss:

\[ \Delta P = \frac{8 \cdot \mu \cdot L \cdot Q}{\pi \cdot R^4} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe (10 m),
- \( Q \) is the volumetric flow rate,
- \( R \) is the radius of the pipe (0.05 m).

To find the volumetric flow rate \( Q \), we can use:

\[ Q = A \cdot v = \pi \cdot R^2 \cdot v \]

Calculating the area:

\[ A = \pi \cdot (0.05)^2 \approx 0.00785 \, m^2 \]

Now, calculating \( Q \):

\[ Q = 0.00785 \cdot 0.01 \approx 0.0000785 \, m^3/s \]

Now we can substitute \( Q \) back into the Hagen-Poiseuille equation to find the pressure loss. 

This approach will give us a clear understanding of the pressure loss in the pipe due to the laminar flow of water."
