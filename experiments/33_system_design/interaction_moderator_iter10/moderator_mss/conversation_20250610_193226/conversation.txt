\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately \( 1000 \, kg/m^3 \)), \( v \) is the velocity (0.01 m/s), \( D \) is the diameter (0.1 m), and \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, Pa \cdot s \)).

Calculating \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{(1000 \, kg/m^3)(0.01 \, m/s)(0.1 \, m)}{0.001 \, Pa \cdot s} = 1000
\]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is indeed laminar. Thus, using the friction factor \( f = \frac{25}{Re} \) is appropriate. Now we can apply the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss \( \Delta P \):

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting our values, we can calculate the pressure loss accurately."

   \[
   \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
   \]

   \[

   \]

   \[

   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 5 = 0.125 \, Pa
   \]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is \( 0.125 \, Pa \). This approach aligns with the principles of fluid dynamics, and I believe we have arrived at a valid solution."
