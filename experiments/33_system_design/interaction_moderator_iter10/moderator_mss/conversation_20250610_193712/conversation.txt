The Reynolds number \( Re \) can be calculated using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \( \rho \) is the density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³), \( v \) is the velocity (0.01 m/s), \( D \) is the diameter (0.1 m), and \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s).

Calculating \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \( Re < 2000 \), the flow is laminar. In laminar flow, the friction factor \( f \) is given by:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Now we can use this value of \( f \) in the Darcy-Weisbach equation to calculate the pressure loss:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Substituting the values:

\[
\Delta P = \left(\frac{64}{1000}\right) \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this will give us the pressure loss in the pipe. I believe this approach will yield a more accurate result than the simplification suggested by Agent 1."
