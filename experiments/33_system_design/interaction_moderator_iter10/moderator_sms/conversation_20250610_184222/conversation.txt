\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the flow regime to calculate the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is quite low (0.01 m/s), we might expect laminar flow. The Reynolds number (\(Re\)) can be calculated as follows:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}\) at room temperature). 

If \(Re < 2000\), the flow is laminar, and the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

If we find that the flow is laminar, we can proceed with the calculations using the values provided. If it turns out to be turbulent (which seems unlikely with the given low velocity), we would need to use a different method to find \(f\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number first to determine the flow regime."
