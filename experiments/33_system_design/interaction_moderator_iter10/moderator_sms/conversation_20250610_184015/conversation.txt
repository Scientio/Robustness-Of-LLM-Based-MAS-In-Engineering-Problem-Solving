\[

\]

where \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss, \(f\) is the Darcy friction factor, \(L\) is the length of the pipe, \(D\) is the diameter, \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid, and \(v\) is the flow velocity.

To proceed, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). This can be calculated using the Moody chart or empirical correlations, depending on whether the flow is laminar or turbulent. Given that the flow velocity is relatively low at 0.01 m/s, we should first check if the flow is laminar, which occurs when the Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\).

The Reynolds number can be calculated using the formula:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water. Assuming standard conditions, the density of water is approximately \(1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\) and the dynamic viscosity is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\).

Let's calculate the Reynolds number to determine the flow regime and subsequently the friction factor."
