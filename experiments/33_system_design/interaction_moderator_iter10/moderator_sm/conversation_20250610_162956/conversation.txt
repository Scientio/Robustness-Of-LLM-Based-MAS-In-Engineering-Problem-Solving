AGENT 1: "To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is a widely accepted method for this purpose. The equation is given by:

\[ \Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2} \]

where:
- \( \Delta P \) is the pressure loss,
- \( f \) is the Darcy-Weisbach friction factor,
- \( L \) is the length of the pipe,
- \( D \) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \( \rho \) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \( v \) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the flow regime, which can be done by calculating the Reynolds number:

\[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu} \]

where \( \mu \) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \( 0.001 \, \text{Pa·s} \) at room temperature). Based on the Reynolds number, we can determine whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will influence the value of the friction factor \( f \).

For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[ f = \frac{64}{Re} \]

For turbulent flow, we can use empirical correlations such as the Colebrook-White equation or the Moody chart to find \( f \).

Once we have the friction factor, we can plug all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation to find the pressure loss. Given the low velocity of 0.01 m/s, it's likely that the flow will be laminar, so we should calculate the Reynolds number first to confirm this before proceeding."
