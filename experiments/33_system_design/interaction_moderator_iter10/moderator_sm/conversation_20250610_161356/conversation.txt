where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the velocity of the fluid.

First, we need to determine the friction factor \(f\). Since the flow velocity is quite low (0.01 m/s), we can assume laminar flow, which occurs when the Reynolds number \(Re < 2000\). The Reynolds number is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \text{ Pa·s}\) at room temperature). 

Substituting the values, we get:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

Since \(Re < 2000\), we confirm laminar flow. For laminar flow, the friction factor can be calculated using:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

Substituting the value of \(Re\):

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now we can substitute all the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation. The length \(L = 10 \text{ m}\), the diameter \(D = 0.1 \text{ m}\), and the velocity \(v = 0.01 \text{ m/s}\):

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating this step-by-step:

1. Calculate \( \frac{10}{0.1} = 100 \).
2. Calculate \( (0.01)^2 = 0.0001 \).
3. Calculate \( 1000 \cdot 0.0001 = 0.1 \).
4. Calculate \( \frac{0.1}{2} = 0.05 \).
5. Finally, \( \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \text{ Pa} \).
