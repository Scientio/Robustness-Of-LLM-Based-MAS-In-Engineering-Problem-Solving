##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

1. **Calculate the Reynolds number (Re):**
   \[
   Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
   \]
   Assuming a dynamic viscosity (\(\mu\)) of water at room temperature is about \(0.001 \, \text{Pa.s}\):
   \[
   Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
   \]

2. **Determine the friction factor (f):** 
   For turbulent flow (Re > 4000) we would use the Moody chart, but since Re = 1000, we are in the laminar regime:
   \[
   f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
   \]

3. **Calculate the pressure loss:**
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
   \]
   \[
   \Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
   \]

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.32 Pa.
