To solve this, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which states:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) is the pressure loss,
- \(f\) is the friction factor,
- \(L\) is the length of the pipe,
- \(D\) is the diameter of the pipe,
- \(\rho\) is the density of the fluid (for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³),
- \(v\) is the flow velocity.

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor \(f\). The Reynolds number \(Re\) is given by:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately 0.001 Pa·s).

After calculating \(Re\), we can find \(f\) using the appropriate correlation (e.g., Moody chart or empirical formulas).

I suggest we start by calculating \(Re\) to find the flow regime and then determine the friction factor. What do you all think? Any suggestions on this approach?
