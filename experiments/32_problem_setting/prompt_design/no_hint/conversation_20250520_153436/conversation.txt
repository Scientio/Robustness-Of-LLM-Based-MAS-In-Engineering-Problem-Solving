Team, we need to calculate the pressure loss in a pipe with a diameter of 0.1 meters and a length of 10 meters, where water is flowing at a velocity of 0.01 m/s. 

To approach this problem, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation, which is given by:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss
- \(f\) = Darcy friction factor (which depends on the flow regime)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number to find the friction factor. The Reynolds number \(Re\) is calculated as:

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Once we have the Reynolds number, we can determine if the flow is laminar or turbulent, which will help us find the appropriate friction factor \(f\).

I suggest we calculate the Reynolds number first, and then proceed with finding the friction factor. What do you think?
