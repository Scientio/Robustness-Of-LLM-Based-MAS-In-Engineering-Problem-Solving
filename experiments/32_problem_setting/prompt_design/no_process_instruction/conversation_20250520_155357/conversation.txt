##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \(Re\) to find the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where \(\mu\) is the dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\) at room temperature).

Substituting the values:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m³}\)
- \(\mu = 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For \(Re = 1000\), the flow is likely in the transitional regime. We can approximate \(f\) using the Moody chart or empirical correlations. For laminar flow (\(Re < 2000\)), \(f\) can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re} = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now substituting back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]

Thus, the pressure loss in the pipe is:

Preliminary Decision: 0.32 Pa
