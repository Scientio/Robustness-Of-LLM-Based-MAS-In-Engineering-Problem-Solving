##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \( \Delta P \) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \( f \) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \( L \) = length of the pipe (m)
- \( D \) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \( \rho \) = density of water (approximately 1000 kg/m³)
- \( v \) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number \( Re \):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \( \mu \) of water is approximately \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \) Pa·s:

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely in the transitional regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to estimate the friction factor \( f \). For laminar flow (Re < 2000), the friction factor can be calculated as:

\[
f = \frac{64}{Re}
\]

So,

\[
f = \frac{64}{1000} = 0.064
\]

Now, substituting the values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

- \( L = 10 \) m
- \( D = 0.1 \) m
- \( \rho = 1000 \) kg/m³
- \( v = 0.01 \) m/s

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating:

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.064 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.32 \, \text{Pa}
\]
