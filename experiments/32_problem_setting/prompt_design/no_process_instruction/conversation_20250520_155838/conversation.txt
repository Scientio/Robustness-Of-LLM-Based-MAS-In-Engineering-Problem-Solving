##### LEAD:
To calculate the pressure loss in a pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, m\)
- \(L = 10 \, m\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, m/s\)
- For water, \(\rho \approx 1000 \, kg/m³\)

First, we need to calculate the Reynolds number \(Re\) to determine the friction factor \(f\):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Assuming the dynamic viscosity \(\mu\) of water is approximately \(0.001 \, Pa \cdot s\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.1}{0.001} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is likely in the transitional regime. We can use the Moody chart or empirical correlations to find \(f\). For turbulent flow, a common approximation is:

\[
f \approx \frac{0.079}{Re^{0.25}} \approx \frac{0.079}{1000^{0.25}} \approx 0.025
\]

Now, substituting values into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

Calculating it step by step:

1. \(\frac{L}{D} = \frac{10}{0.1} = 100\)
2. \((0.01)^2 = 0.0001\)
3. \(\frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.05\)

Thus,

\[
\Delta P = 0.025 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.125 \, Pa
\]

Preliminary Decision: The pressure loss in the pipe is approximately 0.125 Pa.
