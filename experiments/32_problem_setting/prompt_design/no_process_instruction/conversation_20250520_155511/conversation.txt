To calculate the pressure loss in the pipe, we can use the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = f \cdot \frac{L}{D} \cdot \frac{\rho v^2}{2}
\]

Where:
- \(\Delta P\) = pressure loss (Pa)
- \(f\) = friction factor (dimensionless)
- \(L\) = length of the pipe (m)
- \(D\) = diameter of the pipe (m)
- \(\rho\) = density of the fluid (kg/m³) - for water, approximately 1000 kg/m³
- \(v\) = flow velocity (m/s)

First, we need to determine the Reynolds number (\(Re\)) to find the friction factor (\(f\)):

\[
Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\mu}
\]

Where:
- \(\mu\) = dynamic viscosity of water (approximately \(1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{Pa·s}\))

Given:
- \(D = 0.1 \, \text{m}\)
- \(L = 10 \, \text{m}\)
- \(v = 0.01 \, \text{m/s}\)
- \(\rho = 1000 \, \text{kg/m}^3\)

Calculating \(Re\):

\[
Re = \frac{1000 \times 0.01 \times 0.1}{1 \times 10^{-3}} = 1000
\]

For a Reynolds number of 1000, the flow is considered transitional. We can estimate the friction factor using the Blasius equation for smooth pipes:

\[
f = 0.079 \cdot Re^{-0.25} \approx 0.079 \cdot (1000)^{-0.25} \approx 0.079 \cdot 0.1778 \approx 0.014
\]

Now substituting back into the Darcy-Weisbach equation:

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot \frac{10}{0.1} \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot (0.01)^2}{2}
\]

\[
\Delta P = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot \frac{1000 \cdot 0.0001}{2} = 0.014 \cdot 100 \cdot 0.05 = 0.070
